\title{Timing the steep edge: recovering picosecond time resolution from a
saturated sampling calorimeter with a high-gain/low-gain ratio method}

\begin{abstract}
Future high-luminosity collider experiments require electromagnetic calorimeters
that measure not only the energy but also the arrival time of showers to a few tens
of picoseconds. We report time-resolution measurements made with a
tungsten/LYSO:Ce sampling ``shashlik'' calorimeter module (RADiCAL) exposed to
$25$--$150$~GeV electrons at the CERN SPS in May 2023. The high-gain channels used
for timing saturate, clipping at $820$~mV and destroying the pulse peak. We recover
the unclipped rising edge using a second, low-gain copy of each pulse: a
constant-fraction discriminator is placed at a fixed fraction of the
\emph{low-gain-predicted} true peak (\texttt{hg\_lgcfd}), which times an edge a
factor $\sim3.7$ steeper than the clipped-peak foot. Using a microchannel-plate
(MCP)-free differential estimator, $(\mathrm{DW}-\mathrm{UP})/2$, formed from the
four upstream and four downstream silicon-photomultiplier readouts, we measure the
time resolution and its dependence on shower brightness. The resolution improves as
the inverse of the collected light, the signature of slew-rate-limited timing,
reaching $25.4$~ps for the brightest, best-contained $150$~GeV showers and
extrapolating to a floor of $24.7\pm1.1$~ps consistent with an irreducible
shower-depth fluctuation of $\sim\!1\,X_0$. Expressed as a reproducible amplitude
threshold rather than a fixed event count, the resolution falls continuously from
$\sim\!50$~ps for all contained showers toward this floor. Across four detector
builds the resolution tracks the scintillation light yield, confirming that light,
not beam energy, sets the clock. We discuss the implications for detector design and
a depth-corrected estimator as the route below the present floor.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

At the High-Luminosity LHC and proposed future colliders, of order 100--200
simultaneous interactions per bunch crossing overlap in space. Associating energy
deposits with the correct primary vertex, and rejecting out-of-time pile-up,
requires that calorimeters resolve the \emph{time} of a shower to a few tens of
picoseconds in addition to its energy --- so-called 4D, or fast-timing,
calorimetry~\cite{cmsmtd}. Achieving this in a radiation-hard, finely-segmented
electromagnetic calorimeter is the goal of the RADiCAL R\&D
programme~\cite{radical}.

A first RADiCAL test-beam measurement reported a time resolution of $\approx\!27$~ps
at $150$~GeV using a constant-fraction discriminator at $5\%$ of the measured
(clipped) pulse height, with the resolution parametrised as
$\sigma_t = a/\sqrt{E}\oplus b$ and $b=17.5$~ps~\cite{radical}. That measurement is
limited by a feature of the front end: the high-gain (HG) channels optimised for
timing saturate the digitiser well before the shower maximum, so the pulse \emph{peak}
--- and with it the steepest, most informative part of the rising edge --- is never
recorded; nor was the saturated peak recovered. In
this paper we show that the unclipped edge can be reconstructed event by event from
a simultaneously-recorded low-gain (LG) copy of the same pulse, and that timing this
recovered edge improves the resolution and exposes the underlying physics that sets
it. We further show that the resolution is governed by the collected scintillation
light through a slew-rate law, extrapolate the irreducible floor, and identify its
origin as shower-depth fluctuation.

\section{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Detector}
The module under test is a tungsten/LYSO:Ce sampling calorimeter of the ``shashlik''
type: alternating plates of tungsten absorber and cerium-doped lutetium--yttrium
oxyorthosilicate (LYSO:Ce) scintillator, read out longitudinally by wavelength-shifting
capillaries. Four capillaries traverse the stack and are each read out at both ends,
giving $4\times2 = 8$ silicon-photomultiplier (SiPM) channels. We label the four
downstream ends collectively DW and the four upstream ends UP. The transverse
position of each incident electron is measured by a wire chamber and used to define
a fiducial region centred on the beam spot.

Four detector builds were exposed, differing in their scintillator/absorber
composition and hence in light yield: a high-light LYSO build (``DSB1''), a
mixed LYSO/LuAG build (``MIXED''), a low-light LuAG:Ce build (``LUAG''), and a
three-segment LYSO build with an energy section (``TENERGY''). DSB1, the brightest,
is used for the headline measurements; all four are compared in
Section~\ref{sec:cross}.

\subsection{Readout and digitisation}
Each capillary end is read by a SiPM whose signal is split into a high-gain (HG)
copy, optimised for timing, and a low-gain (LG) copy, optimised for energy linearity.
All channels are digitised by DRS4 switched-capacitor waveform digitisers
(CAEN~DT5742)~\cite{drs4} sampling at $5$~GS/s over a 1024-cell window, with per-cell
timing and amplitude calibration applied offline. The HG channels clip at
$\approx\!820$~mV; the LG channels remain linear across the full energy range.

\subsection{Beam and timing reference}
The measurements were performed in a $25$--$150$~GeV electron beam at the CERN SPS in
May 2023. A microchannel-plate photomultiplier (MCP-PMT) upstream of the module
provides a common start signal; all per-channel crossing times in the reduced data
are expressed relative to this MCP time. As shown below, our differential estimator
cancels this common reference, so the MCP jitter does not enter the quoted
resolutions.

\section{Saturation and the HG/LG-ratio timing method}
\label{sec:method}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.86\linewidth]{clip.png}
\caption{Distribution of recorded high-gain pulse-peak amplitudes (one channel,
$125$~GeV). The pile-up at $\approx\!820$~mV is the digitiser clip: most showers
saturate, so the true peak --- and the steep edge that leads to it --- is not
directly recorded.}
\label{fig:clip}
\end{figure}

The difficulty is illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:clip}: for all but the smallest
showers the HG pulse rises into the $820$~mV clip and piles up there, so the recorded
waveform is flat across its maximum. A constant-fraction discriminator (CFD)
referenced to the \emph{measured} peak is then forced onto the shallow ``foot'' of the
pulse, where the slope --- and therefore the timing information --- is poor.

The LG copy, although too small for precise timing on its own, remains linear and
therefore measures the true pulse amplitude that the HG channel \emph{would} have
reached. We exploit this with a per-end calibration
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{HG}_\mathrm{true} = a + b\,\mathrm{LG}_\mathrm{peak},
  \label{eq:hglg}
\end{equation}
determined from the unsaturated population (Fig.~\ref{fig:hglg}), which predicts the
unclipped HG peak event by event. A CFD threshold is then placed at a fixed fraction
($15\%$) of this predicted true peak and applied to the \emph{recorded} HG waveform.
Because the predicted peak lies far above the clip, this threshold sits on the steep,
unsaturated portion of the rising edge rather than on the foot. We denote the
resulting MCP-referenced crossing time \texttt{hg\_lgcfd}.

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.96\linewidth]{hglg.png}
\caption{High-gain versus low-gain pulse-peak amplitude for the eight readout channels
($100$~GeV). The high-gain response saturates at $820$~mV (horizontal band) while the
low-gain copy remains linear; the per-channel linear fit (red line), Eq.~(\ref{eq:hglg}),
predicts the unclipped true peak event by event.}
\label{fig:hglg}
\end{figure*}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{pulse.png}
\caption{A single saturated high-gain waveform (one channel, $125$~GeV). The recorded
pulse clips at $820$~mV (grey); the low-gain copy predicts a true peak of $1997$~mV
(green). Placing the CFD at $15\%$ of the predicted peak (\texttt{hg\_lgcfd}, blue)
times an edge of $381$~mV/ns, against only $102$~mV/ns at the clipped-peak foot where
the $5\%$ CFD (\texttt{cfd05}, red) is forced to sit --- a factor $3.7$ steeper.}
\label{fig:pulse}
\end{figure}

The benefit is direct (Fig.~\ref{fig:pulse}): the recovered edge is timed where the
local slope is $381$~mV/ns, against $102$~mV/ns at the clipped-peak foot --- a factor
$\sim\!3.7$. Since the slew contribution to the time resolution scales as
$\sigma_t \simeq \sigma_V/(\mathrm{d}V/\mathrm{d}t)$ with $\sigma_V$ the voltage noise,
this steeper edge translates directly into better timing.

\section{Timing estimator and event selection}
\label{sec:estimator}

For each event we form the per-end mean crossing time from the channels with a valid
pulse, and combine the two ends into the differential estimator
\begin{equation}
  t_{\mathrm{DW-UP}} \equiv \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(\bar t_\mathrm{DW} - \bar t_\mathrm{UP}\right),
  \label{eq:dwup}
\end{equation}
where $\bar t_\mathrm{DW}$ and $\bar t_\mathrm{UP}$ average up to four downstream and
four upstream SiPM times respectively. The difference cancels any time common to both
ends --- in particular the MCP start --- so $t_{\mathrm{DW-UP}}$ is free of the
reference jitter; averaging four channels per end suppresses uncorrelated
single-channel noise by up to a factor of two. The complementary sum
$(\mathrm{DW}+\mathrm{UP})/2$, which retains the common reference, is used only as a
cross-check. The single-event time resolution quoted throughout is the standard
deviation of a Gaussian fit to the \emph{central peak} of the $t_{\mathrm{DW-UP}}$
distribution; the small non-Gaussian shoulder from mis-measured low-amplitude
channels is excluded from the fit, as in Ref.~\cite{radical}.

Events are required to fall within the wire-chamber fiducial region and to have a
valid MCP signal. To suppress poorly-contained showers, whose low light makes them
non-representative outliers, we require at least two of the four ends on each side to
have a good pulse and reject events whose total low-gain signal $\Sigma\mathrm{LG}$
(our amplitude/brightness estimator) falls below $40\%$ of the per-energy median.

A central theme of this paper is the dependence of $\sigma_t$ on
$\Sigma\mathrm{LG}$. Because shower fluctuations spread the collected light at fixed
beam energy, $\Sigma\mathrm{LG}$ and beam energy are distinct: the brightest events
at one energy overlap the typical events at the next. We therefore analyse the
resolution as a function of brightness directly.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Timing distributions}
Figure~\ref{fig:dist} shows, for DSB1 with all six energies pooled and binned by
$\Sigma\mathrm{LG}$, the $t_{\mathrm{DW-UP}}$ (top) and $(\mathrm{DW}+\mathrm{UP})/2$
(middle) distributions together with their central-peak Gaussian fits, and the
fitted $\sigma_t$ versus amplitude (bottom). The distributions are clean,
single-peaked and well described by the fits at every amplitude; the differential
estimator is consistently narrower than the absolute one, as expected once the
common-mode reference jitter is removed. The fitted width falls monotonically with
brightness.

\subsection{Resolution versus brightness: the slew law}
\label{sec:slew}
Slicing each beam energy into consecutive groups of 1000 events ordered by brightness
yields the per-energy resolution curves of Fig.~\ref{fig:res}. Two features stand
out. First, where the energies overlap in $\Sigma\mathrm{LG}$ they fall on a
\emph{single} curve: the time resolution is set by the collected light, not by the
beam energy. Second, $\sigma_t$ falls faster than the $1/\sqrt{\Sigma\mathrm{LG}}$
expected from photostatistics; it scales as $1/\Sigma\mathrm{LG}$, the signature of
slew-rate-limited timing, since the edge slope is proportional to amplitude. We
therefore fit
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_t(\Sigma\mathrm{LG}) = \sqrt{\left(\frac{a}{\Sigma\mathrm{LG}}\right)^{\!2} + b^2},
  \label{eq:slew}
\end{equation}
to the brightest (best-measured) slice of each energy, where $b$ is the irreducible
floor as $\Sigma\mathrm{LG}\to\infty$.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{res.png}
\caption{DSB1 resolution in consecutive 1000-event brightness slices, per beam energy.
The curves collapse onto one $\sigma_t(\Sigma\mathrm{LG})$ law. The slew fit of
Eq.~(\ref{eq:slew}) to the brightest slice of each energy (open circles) gives a floor
$b = 24.7\pm1.1$~ps; the brightest 1000 of $150$~GeV ($\star$) is the $25.4$~ps
headline.}
\label{fig:res}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Resolution versus amplitude threshold}
\label{sec:thresh}
A fixed ``brightest-$N$'' selection is convenient but depends on the size of the data
set. The reproducible, experiment-independent quantity is the resolution of all events
above an amplitude \emph{threshold}. Selecting the brightest 1000 events is identical
to one such cut (here $\Sigma\mathrm{LG} > 5135$). Scanning the threshold gives the
cumulative curve of Fig.~\ref{fig:thresh}: as the cut is raised, only brighter,
better-timed showers survive and $\sigma_t$ slides from $\sim\!50$~ps for all
contained $150$~GeV showers toward the floor. The amplitude cut alone improves the
resolution by roughly a factor of two. The headline number is thus one point on a
continuous curve rather than a special selection.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{thresh.png}
\caption{DSB1 resolution versus amplitude threshold: $\sigma_t$ of all events with
$\Sigma\mathrm{LG} > X$, per energy. Raising the cut keeps brighter showers and the
resolution approaches the floor. The ``brightest 1000'' headline is the single point
at $\Sigma\mathrm{LG}>5135$ ($\star$).}
\label{fig:thresh}
\end{figure}

\subsection{The floor}
\label{sec:floor}
The slew fit, Eq.~(\ref{eq:slew}), yields $b = 24.7\pm1.1$~ps for DSB1. An independent
extrapolation of $\sigma_t$ versus beam energy gives a consistent
$\sim\!23$~ps. Crucially, the data do not exhibit a measured plateau: the resolution
is still falling, if gently, at the brightest events, so the floor is an
extrapolation and its true uncertainty exceeds the fit error. Three estimates
nonetheless converge: the amplitude fit ($24.7$~ps), the energy fit ($\sim\!23$~ps),
and the physical expectation from shower-depth fluctuation discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:discuss} ($\sim\!28$~ps, i.e.\ $\sim\!1\,X_0$). The convergence on
$\sim\!23$--$28$~ps makes a substantially lower floor unlikely.

The earlier $\sigma_t = a/\sqrt{E}\oplus b$ parametrisation of Ref.~\cite{radical}
returns a lower floor, $b=17.5$~ps. That form assumes a photostatistics
($1/\sqrt{E}$) stochastic term; the data of Section~\ref{sec:slew}, however, fall as
$1/\Sigma\mathrm{LG}$ (slew-rate limited), more steeply than $1/\sqrt{\,}$. A
$1/\sqrt{E}$ fit forced through such data compensates with an artificially low floor.
The slew interpretation, which matches the observed scaling, assigns more of the
measured resolution to the irreducible term and raises the floor to $\sim\!25$~ps,
identifying it with shower-depth fluctuation (Section~\ref{sec:discuss}).

\subsection{Cross-build comparison: light yield sets the clock}
\label{sec:cross}
Applying the same analysis to all four builds (Fig.~\ref{fig:cross}) orders them
cleanly by scintillation light yield. The best-achievable (brightest-slice) resolution
at $150$~GeV is $25.4$~ps for high-light LYSO (DSB1), $28.7$~ps for the
LYSO+energy build (TENERGY), $40.0$~ps for the mixed build (MIXED), and $41.2$~ps for
low-light LuAG (LUAG); the corresponding slew floors are $24.7$, $28.1$, $40.2$ and
$36.0$~ps. More light gives a faster edge and better timing, exactly as the slew
argument predicts. (For the low-light builds a fixed-threshold leading-edge
discriminator is the most robust timing source, the LG-ratio CFD being susceptible to
the small pulses; for the LYSO-rich builds \texttt{hg\_lgcfd} is used.)

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discuss}

The measurements show that, with the rising edge recovered, RADiCAL timing is
slew-rate limited over the entire accessible range: $\sigma_t \propto
1/\Sigma\mathrm{LG}$, and the resolution is a function of collected light alone. At
the brightest events the slew term has fallen to only $\sim\!5$~ps and the floor
dominates, so additional light yields rapidly diminishing returns --- a doubling of
light would improve $\sigma_t$ by only a few tenths of a picosecond.

What, then, is the floor? It cannot be electronics jitter alone, which the
four-channel-per-end average already suppresses. The differential estimator
$(\mathrm{DW}-\mathrm{UP})/2$ is, by construction, sensitive to the longitudinal
position of the shower: light from a deeper shower reaches the downstream end earlier
and the upstream end later, shifting the two in opposite senses. Shower-to-shower
fluctuation in the depth of energy deposition therefore produces an irreducible spread
in $t_{\mathrm{DW-UP}}$ that does \emph{not} decrease with light, because it is common
to all channels at an end and geometric rather than statistical in origin. Its scale,
$\sim\!1\,X_0$ of path-length difference, is $\sim\!25$--$28$~ps, matching the fitted
floor. The very feature that makes the estimator MCP-free --- its differential
construction --- is also what couples it to shower depth.

This identification points to the route below the floor. The same channel-to-channel
light-sharing that carries the depth information could be used to estimate each
shower's depth and subtract its timing bias event by event. Such a depth-corrected
estimator is the natural next step and is the subject of ongoing work.

%======================================================================
\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

We have demonstrated that saturation of the timing front end in a W/LYSO sampling
calorimeter need not limit its time resolution. By predicting the unclipped pulse peak
from a linear low-gain copy and timing a constant fraction of that predicted peak, the
steep rising edge lost to clipping is recovered, improving on the clipped-peak result.
Using an MCP-free differential estimator we measure a best-achievable resolution of
$25.4$~ps for $150$~GeV electrons in the high-light LYSO build, and we show that the
resolution is slew-rate limited, scaling as the inverse of the collected light and
depending on shower brightness rather than beam energy. The resolution extrapolates to
a floor of $\approx\!25$~ps, consistent across amplitude- and energy-based
extrapolations and with the expected shower-depth fluctuation of $\sim\!1\,X_0$. Across
four builds the resolution tracks the scintillation light yield. The dominant
remaining limitation is the shower itself; a depth-corrected estimator offers a path
beyond it.
