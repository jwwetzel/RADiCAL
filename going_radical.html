A test beam is a controlled rehearsal: take a known particle beam, point it at a prototype detector, and measure exactly how the detector responds. Here we shot electrons into RADiCAL, a prototype calorimeter, to see how precisely it measures a particle's energy and its arrival time.

Test beam the setup

What: a particle beam of known type & energy, steered down a beamline onto your detector.

Here: CERN SPS, H2 line — electrons from 25 to 150 GeV, a few per spill.

⚡ "GeV" = energy. 1 GeV ≈ the rest-energy of a proton; a 150 GeV e⁻ is ultra-relativistic.

Why timing? motivation

What: future colliders pile ~100s of collisions into one beam crossing.

Here: tag each particle's time to a few picoseconds and you can untangle which collision it came from — "4D" calorimetry.

⚡ Target of CMS's timing upgrade: ~30 ps. We reach 27 ps.

The whole chain one breath

What: beam → trigger scintillators (decide "record this") → MCP (start the clock) → wire chambers (where did it hit) → RADiCAL (energy + time) → cables → digitizer → laptop → analysis.

Here: every box in the diagram above is one link in this chain.

⚡ Two paths: a fast trigger (NIM logic) that says "go," and the signals that get recorded.

What we measured the result

What: energy resolution (how well we know E) and time resolution (how well we know when).

Here: best time resolution 27 ps at 150 GeV using a clever reference-free trick (see corner difference).

⚡ 27 ps is the time for light to cross ~8 mm. We measure time better than that.

Particles are invisible. A detector's only job is to turn a particle into something you can measure — usually light or charge — without disturbing the measurement you care about.

Calorimetry

Calorimeter detector

What: a block that absorbs a particle entirely and measures the energy it dumps.

Here: RADiCAL is an electromagnetic calorimeter — built to stop electrons & photons.

⚡ Energy ∝ amount of light produced. More energy → bigger pulse.

EM shower physics

What: a high-energy e⁻/γ doesn't stop at once — it cascades: bremsstrahlung → pair-production → more e⁻/γ, doubling until the energy runs out.

Here: the calorimeter samples this shower; its depth/width set containment.

⚡ Radiation length (X₀): the depth scale of a shower. Tungsten X₀ ≈ 3.5 mm — very compact.

Sampling calorimeter design

What: alternate dense absorber (makes the shower) with scintillator (samples the light). Cheaper/compact vs a solid crystal.

Here: RADiCAL samples; only a fraction of the shower makes light → a "sampling" term in the resolution.

⚡ Trade-off: sampling = compact & cheap, but worse energy resolution than homogeneous.

RADiCAL our detector

What: a shashlik module of tungsten (absorber) + LYSO (scintillator) plates, read out by wavelength-shifting capillaries to SiPMs.

Here: ~14×14 mm active face; 8 readout channels (4 corners × 2 depths). Designed for radiation-hard, fast, compact calorimetry.

⚡ Two gains per corner: HG (high-gain, at shower max → timing) and LG (low-gain, full length → energy).

Shashlik geometry

What: a stack of absorber/scintillator plates with fibers threaded through holes ("shashlik" = skewered kebab).

Here: the capillaries run longitudinally through the W/LYSO stack to pipe light out the back.

LYSO & tungsten materials

What: LYSO = a fast, dense, bright scintillating crystal. Tungsten (W) = very dense absorber.

Here: LYSO's speed (~40 ns) helps timing; W's density makes the module short.

⚡ "Fast + dense + bright" is the holy trinity for a timing calorimeter.

Turning particles into a signal

Scintillation light

What: certain materials emit a flash of light when ionizing radiation passes through.

Here: the LYSO scintillates; that light is the energy signal.

WLS capillary / fiber light pipe

What: a wavelength-shifting fiber absorbs scintillation light and re-emits it at a longer wavelength that travels down the fiber to a sensor.

Here: 8 capillaries collect light from the shower and guide it to the SiPMs.

⚡ "Capillary" here = a thin WLS light guide running through the module.

SiPM photosensor

What: a silicon photomultiplier — a grid of tiny avalanche diodes in Geiger mode. One photon → an avalanche → a measurable pulse.

Here: reads the capillary light into an electrical pulse. Compact, magnetic-field-immune, gain ~10⁶.

⚡ Biased just above breakdown ("over-voltage"); each fired cell gives ~the same charge.

MCP / MCP-PMT timing reference

What: a micro-channel plate photomultiplier — a thin plate pierced by millions of tiny channels, each a continuous electron multiplier. Ultra-fast.

Here: the MCP gives the precise time-zero (t₀) against which everything else is timed.

⚡ MCP single-hit timing ~tens of ps. Its jitter cancels in our corner trick.

Wire chamber tracking

What: a gas detector that reports where the particle crossed. This one is a delay-line type: position = difference of pulse arrival times at the two ends of a line.

Here: gives (x, y) of each track so we can map the detector and cut on beam position.

⚡ Self-calibration: the sum of the two end-times is position-independent → it measures the resolution. We get ~3.6 mm.

Pb-glass leakage catcher

What: lead-glass blocks that radiate Cherenkov light when a shower passes. A simple Cherenkov calorimeter.

Here: sits behind/beside RADiCAL to catch energy that leaks out the small module.

⚡ Cherenkov light = the optical "sonic boom" of a charged particle faster than light-in-the-medium.

Trigger scintillator beam tag

What: a small scintillator paddle/finger read by a PMT — fires when a particle passes.

Here: a 1×1 cm "finger" defines a clean beam particle; its coincidence makes the trigger.

⚡ Used as a cut later: require the finger fired → reject events with no clean particle.

Every detector outputs a tiny voltage pulse on a cable. Before you can analyze it you have to understand its shape, how it travels, and the connectors that carry it.

The pulse

Pulse the raw signal

What: voltage vs time. Key features: baseline (flat), a fast edge, a peak (amplitude), then a tail.

Here: our SiPM/MCP pulses go negative; "amplitude" = baseline − minimum.

⚡ Two numbers matter: amplitude (∝ peak rate of light) and charge = area (∝ energy).

Pedestal / baseline reference

What: the voltage with no signal. You measure it from early samples and subtract it.

Here: averaged over the first ~50 samples of every waveform before finding the pulse.

Charge integral energy proxy

What: the area under the pulse, ∫V dt. Proportional to the total light = the energy.

Here: the low-gain (LG) charge is our energy estimator; ΣLG maps the shower.

Rise time & slew rate timing

What: how fast the edge climbs. Slew rate = dV/dt on the edge.

Here: timing precision ≈ (electronic noise) ÷ (slew rate). Faster edge → better time.

⚡ This is why fast scintillator + fast sensor = good timing.

Cables & connectors

Coax & 50 Ω transmission

What: a coaxial cable (center conductor + shield) carries fast pulses. Its characteristic impedance is 50 Ω in physics.

Here: every fast signal rides 50 Ω coax into the digitizer.

⚡ Match impedances or you get reflections — an echo of the pulse bouncing back.

Reflection / termination gotcha

What: at any impedance mismatch, part of the pulse reflects. A 50 Ω terminator absorbs it.

Here: the delay-line wire chamber uses propagation time; everywhere else reflections are noise to avoid.

BNC connector connector

What: a bayonet (twist-lock) coax connector — the workhorse of lab electronics.

Here: the MCP's signal BNC sits in the beam — beam hitting it pre-showers, making the ring we see in the maps.

⚡ A connector body is ~1 radiation length of metal → it starts EM showers if it's in the beam.

SMA connector connector

What: a small screw-on coax connector (sub-miniature, version A). Smaller than BNC, good to high frequency.

Here: the MCP's high-voltage SMA is the off-center connector ring in the maps.

LEMO & HV connector

What: LEMO = push-pull connector common on NIM modules. HV = high-voltage line that biases PMTs/MCPs/SiPMs.

Here: NIM logic signals hop between modules on LEMO; HV powers the sensors.

Analog pulses are messy. NIM electronics turn them into clean yes/no logic for triggering, and a digitizer records the full waveform for analysis.

NIM trigger logic

NIM standard the crate

What: Nuclear Instrumentation Module — a 1960s mechanical + electrical standard. Modules slot into a powered crate/bin.

Here: the trigger is built from a few NIM modules wired together.

⚡ NIM logic levels: logic-0 = 0 V, logic-1 = −0.8 V (into 50 Ω). Fast (ns) and simple.

Discriminator analog → logic

What: outputs a clean fixed-width logic pulse only if the input crosses a set threshold.

Here: turns each scintillator's messy pulse into a crisp "a particle was here" bit.

⚡ Threshold rejects small noise; the output time/width is standardized.

Leading-edge & time-walk the problem

What: a simple discriminator fires when the rising edge crosses a fixed threshold. Big pulses reach it sooner than small ones.

Here: this amplitude-dependent timing error is time-walk — the enemy of precision timing.

⚡ Same particle time, different pulse height → different measured time. Bad.

CFD the fix

What: a constant-fraction discriminator fires at a fixed fraction of each pulse's own peak (e.g. 5%), not a fixed voltage.

Here: the fraction point lands at the same place on the edge regardless of amplitude → kills time-walk. We use CFD-5%.

⚡ We do CFD in software on the recorded waveform — same idea, done offline.

Coincidence (AND) logic

What: a module that outputs a pulse only if two (or more) inputs overlap in time.

Here: require scint-A AND scint-B → a real particle went through both → suppress noise & stray hits.

OR gate logic

What: outputs a pulse if any input fires.

Here: e.g. "fire if any veto counter hit," or to fan signals together.

⚡ AND/OR/NOT + delays = the whole trigger decision, in nanoseconds.

Trigger "record this!"

What: the logic signal that tells the DAQ to capture an event.

Here: beam scintillators in coincidence form the trigger that gates the digitizer.

⚡ No trigger = no data. The trigger defines what "an event" even is.

Digitization (DAQ)

Digitizer / DRS4 record the wave

What: instead of just a time or charge, a waveform digitizer records the whole pulse shape. The CAEN DT5742 uses the DRS4 chip.

Here: we keep the full pulse so we can do CFD, charge, and timing offline, any way we like.

⚡ DRS4 = a switched-capacitor array: 1024 capacitors sample the voltage in turn at 5 GS/s.

Sampling rate 5 GS/s

What: samples per second. 5 GS/s = one sample every 0.2 ns.

Here: fine enough to resolve LYSO/MCP edges; we interpolate between samples for sub-sample timing.

Stop cell / timebase DRS4 quirk

What: the DRS4's capacitors aren't perfectly evenly spaced in time, and sampling stops at a rotating "stop cell."

Here: we calibrate this timebase so it doesn't fake a timing error; it cancels in the corner trick anyway.

ADC & TDC classic readout

What: ADC = analog-to-digital (measures charge/amplitude). TDC = time-to-digital (measures a time).

Here: a waveform digitizer is effectively a fast ADC at every time-slice — it gives you both at once.

Now the physics: turning recorded waveforms into resolutions, and the tricks that make a small detector measure time to a picosecond.

Time resolution (jitter) the headline

What: measure the same kind of event many times; the spread (σ) of the measured time is the resolution.

Here: quoted as a Gaussian σ in picoseconds. Best: 27 ps at 150 GeV.

⚡ 1 ps = 10⁻¹² s. Light goes 0.3 mm in 1 ps — we beat that.

Why you need a reference key idea

What: you can't measure "absolute" time — only a difference between two clocks. Resolution always involves a reference.

Here: the MCP is the reference clock; but its own jitter then pollutes the measurement…

(DW−UP)/2 corner trick the clever bit

What: take the difference of two channels that share the same reference. The common reference time cancels in the subtraction.

Here: Down-corner minus Up-corner, ÷2 → an MCP-free time estimator. This is how we get 27 ps.

⚡ Same idea kills the DRS4 timebase error and the beam-arrival jitter too. Subtraction is powerful.

Energy resolution σE/E

What: spread in measured energy. Parameterized σE/E = a/√E ⊕ b: a stochastic term (sampling/statistics) and a constant term (leakage, calibration).

Here: a short prototype leaks energy → a big constant term; expected for a 14 mm module.

⚡ "⊕" means add in quadrature: a⊕b = √(a²+b²).

Gaussian fit & σ how we quote it

What: histogram the measurements, fit a Gaussian; its width σ is the resolution. We fit the core to avoid tails.

Here: every "ps" and "%" number in the report is a fitted σ.

Time-walk (again) watch for it

What: the amplitude-dependent timing bias. Fix it with CFD, or correct it offline using the measured amplitude.

Here: CFD-5% removes most; a residual "walk correction" cleans the rest.

Fiducial cut selection

What: only keep events whose track hit a clean region of the detector.

Here: a radius cut around the module center (found from the calorimeter edges, not the beam) keeps well-contained showers.

Energy binning / containment selection

What: sort events by how much energy they deposited; the best-contained showers give the best resolution.

Here: the headline uses the best-contained bin — the standard method — disclosed with its efficiency.

Cross-validation honesty

What: pick your selection on one half of the data, measure on the other half. Stops you from fooling yourself by tuning on noise.

Here: the best-bin choice is validated out-of-sample → the 27 ps is real, not a lucky fluctuation.

⚡ Golden rule: never quote a number from the same data you optimized on.

Pencil & paper

The picosecond ruler ★ warm-up

Our best time resolution is 27 ps. How far does light travel in that time? Why does that number make 27 ps feel "small"?

Hint
c ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s = 0.3 mm/ps.
Solution
0.3 mm/ps × 27 ps ≈ 8 mm. We pin a particle's arrival time better than the time it takes light to cross a fingernail — and better than the size of the detector itself. That's why ps timing is hard: every cable length, every mm of geometry matters.

Is the module deep enough? ★ warm-up

Tungsten's radiation length is X₀ ≈ 3.5 mm and the module is ~14 mm deep. How many X₀ is that? A 150 GeV shower peaks around 6–7 X₀ — what does that tell you about energy containment?

Hint
Depth in X₀ = 14 mm ÷ 3.5 mm.
Solution
14/3.5 = 4 X₀ — the module ends before the shower even reaches its maximum (~6–7 X₀). So a lot of energy leaks out the back. That's exactly why the energy resolution has a large constant term: it's a short, leakage-dominated prototype, not a full-depth calorimeter.

Why CFD beats time-walk ★★ reason it out

Two pulses have the same shape but one is twice as tall: V(t) = A·f(t) with different A. Show that the time at a fixed fraction of the peak is the same for both, while the time at a fixed voltage threshold is not.

Hint
Set V(t) = (fraction)·A·f(peak) vs V(t) = (fixed volts), and ask which one still depends on A.
Solution
Fixed fraction φ: solve A·f(t) = φ·A·fpeak → f(t) = φ·fpeak. A cancels — the crossing time depends only on the shape, not the height. Fixed voltage V₀: solve A·f(t) = V₀ → f(t) = V₀/A, which depends on A → bigger pulses cross earlier = time-walk. CFD = timing at a constant fraction → amplitude-independent.

The corner trick ★★ algebra

Each channel measures its time relative to the MCP: tD = TD − TMCP and tU = TU − TMCP. Show the MCP drops out of (tD − tU). Why is that a big deal?

Solution
(tD − tU) = (TD − TMCP) − (TU − TMCP) = TD − TU. The MCP time — and all its jitter — cancels exactly because it's common to both. So you can measure the detector's intrinsic timing without being limited by your reference clock. (The same cancellation kills the DRS4 timebase error and beam-arrival jitter.)

Quadrature & the ÷2 ★★ statistics

Two independent channels each have timing jitter σ. What's the jitter of their difference? Of their average? Why is the headline estimator (tD−tU)/2?

Hint
Independent errors add in quadrature: σ²sum = σ²₁ + σ²₂.
Solution
Difference: √(σ²+σ²) = √2·σ. Average (or here, /2 of the difference): √2·σ / 2 = σ/√2. So dividing the corner difference by 2 turns the √2 penalty of subtracting into a √2 improvement over a single channel — you both cancel the reference and average two measurements.

Noise, slew & jitter ★★ estimate

Timing jitter ≈ (electronic noise) ÷ (slew rate on the edge). If the noise is 2 mV and the pulse rises at 100 mV/ns, what's the jitter? What two things would you change to improve it?

Solution
σt ≈ 2 mV / (100 mV/ns) = 0.02 ns = 20 ps. Improve it by (1) lowering noise and (2) increasing slew rate — i.e. a faster, brighter pulse (fast scintillator, more light, bigger sensor signal). This is the whole game of timing detectors.

Reading the energy resolution ★★★ put it together

Energy resolution is σE/E = a/√E ⊕ b. Take a = 25%·√GeV and b = 11%. Compute σE/E at 25 GeV and at 150 GeV. Which term dominates at high energy, and why?

Hint
⊕ means √(·²+·²). At 25 GeV: a/√E = 25/5 = 5%.
Solution
25 GeV: √(5² + 11²) ≈ 12.1%. 150 GeV: a/√E = 25/√150 ≈ 2.0%, so √(2.0² + 11²) ≈ 11.2%. The constant term b dominates at high E because the stochastic term shrinks as 1/√E while b doesn't. For this short module, b is large (leakage) — see the containment exercise above.

At the keyboard (real data)

Find the module centre from edges ★★ ROOT

Project the calorimeter energy vs track-x in a central y-band. You get a flat-topped plateau with sharp shoulders — the module edges. Read the half-max edges; the midpoint is the centre. Compare to (6.6, 4.7) mm.

// in ROOT, on the 150 GeV ntuple root -l Analysis/Output/150GeV/ntuple.root rad->Draw("sum_lg:x_trk>>px(90,-15,30)", "wc_ok && abs(y_trk-4.7)<4","prof") // repeat with y_trk for the y centre
What to look for
The profile is ~flat across the module and drops steeply at the edges. Find where it falls to half its plateau height on the left and right; the average of those two x-values is the centre. You should land near x ≈ 6.6 mm (y ≈ 4.7 mm). This is beam-independent — the edges are geometry, not where the beam happened to sit.

The beam profile ★ ROOT

Histogram the track positions. How wide is the beam (σ)? Is it centred on the module, or steered off to one side?

rad->Draw("y_trk:x_trk>>h(120,-15,30,120,-20,20)","wc_ok","colz") rad->Draw("x_trk>>hx","wc_ok") // then: hx->Fit("gaus")
Solution
The 2-D plot shows a blob ~7 mm wide in x, ~5 mm in y. Its centroid sits a couple of mm off the module centre (the beam was steered ~2.5 mm toward +x). Lesson: the beam centre ≠ the module centre — which is why we find the module from its edges, not from where the beam landed.

See the MCP cancel ★★ ROOT

Each hg_cfd05[i] is a channel's CFD-5% time minus the MCP (in ns). Fit one channel's time → that width includes the MCP jitter. Now fit the difference of a Down and an Up readout of the same corner — the MCP should cancel and the peak should sharpen.

// single channel (includes MCP jitter): rad->Draw("hg_cfd05[0]>>h1(200,-1,1)", "wc_ok && hg_cfd05[0]>-100") // h1->Fit("gaus") // Down minus Up of the NW corner (MCP cancels): rad->Draw("(hg_cfd05[0]-hg_cfd05[4])>>h2(200,-1,1)", "wc_ok && hg_cfd05[0]>-100 && hg_cfd05[4]>-100")
What to look for
Fit each with a Gaussian; σ is in ns (×1000 → ps). The single-channel width carries the MCP jitter. The difference cancels the common MCP term — its width reflects the two channels' own jitter (divide by √2 for per-channel). This is the corner trick from the algebra exercise, in real data.

Why the wire-chamber sum works ★★★ explain + run

A delay-line wire chamber gives position from the difference of two end-times, x ∝ (tR − tL). Argue that the sum (tR + tL) is independent of where the hit was — and therefore that its spread measures the timing resolution. Then check the number with our macro.

Solution + run it
A hit at position x sends a pulse to both ends; if v is the propagation speed, tR = t₀ + (L/2+x)/v and tL = t₀ + (L/2−x)/v. The difference = 2x/v (position), but the sum = 2t₀ + L/v — no x. So the sum's spread is pure timing noise → it gives the resolution without needing a second tracker. Run it: root -l -b -q 'Analysis/wireChamberResolution.C+' You should get σx ≈ 3.6 mm — coarse because the wire-chamber pulses are timed on a slow digitiser, which is fine for a fiducial cut over a ~7 mm beam.

Part I
The apparatus
How a particle becomes a number — detectors, fast logic, electronics, and the calorimeter. Each chapter ends with a question; answer it before moving on.
CHAPTER 1

Seeing a particle that you can't see

A charged particle is invisible. To detect it you must trick it into leaving a mark you can measure — a flash of light — and then amplify that flash until it's an electrical pulse big enough to act on. That is the photomultiplier-and-scintillator pair, the oldest and most important duet in particle physics.

Scintillators: turning a particle into light foundational

Some materials — certain crystals, plastics, and noble gases — glow faintly when a charged particle passes through them. The particle dumps a little energy as it goes; the material's molecules soak it up, get briefly excited, and then relax by emitting photons. We call this scintillation, and a block of such material a scintillator.

Two properties make a scintillator good. It should be bright (many photons per unit of deposited energy, so small signals are still visible) and fast (the flash should rise and fade quickly, so two particles close in time don't blur together). A plastic paddle flashes in a few nanoseconds and is cheap; a dense crystal like LYSO is brighter and still fast, which is exactly what you want when you care about timing.

Key idea

A detector never measures the particle directly. It measures a proxy — here, light — that the particle leaves behind. Every number you ever quote is really a measurement of that proxy.

The photomultiplier tube: turning light into a pulse foundational

A scintillation flash is tiny — sometimes only a handful of photons. No ordinary circuit can see that. The photomultiplier tube (PMT) solves the problem with an avalanche. Light enters through a window and strikes a photocathode, a coating that releases an electron when a photon lands on it (the photoelectric effect). That single photoelectron is then accelerated by a voltage onto a metal plate called a dynode, where it knocks loose several more electrons. Those are accelerated to the next dynode, which multiplies them again, and so on down a chain of ten or twelve stages.

Each stage multiplies by three or four; ten stages in series multiply by a million. One photon at the front becomes a measurable spike of charge at the anode at the back — a voltage pulse on a cable you can finally work with.

In RADiCAL

The calorimeter itself doesn't use a vacuum PMT — its light is read by compact SiPMs (silicon photomultipliers, the same avalanche idea in solid state). But the experiment's timing reference is an MCP-PMT, a photomultiplier whose dynode chain is replaced by a plate drilled with millions of microscopic channels — the same multiplication, but so fast it pins a time to tens of picoseconds.

Why it needs high voltage foundational

The multiplication only happens because each electron is accelerated before it hits the next dynode — it has to arrive with enough energy to knock several electrons loose. That acceleration comes from a high voltage (often 1–2 kV) split across the dynode chain by a resistor divider, so each stage sits a few hundred volts above the last.

The gain depends steeply on that voltage: turn the HV up and every stage multiplies harder, so the overall gain can change by a factor of two for a 10% change in voltage. That sensitivity is a knob — it's how you set a PMT's gain — but it's also why the HV supply must be stable and why two tubes at the same voltage rarely have the same gain.

Key idea

Gain is not a fixed property of a tube; it's a function of the high voltage you choose. Before a PMT means anything quantitative, you have to find the voltage where it behaves the way you want.

A counter that lies: dark pulses & false triggers building up

Glue a scintillator to a PMT and you have a scintillation counter: a particle passes, light flashes, the tube barks out a pulse, you count it. Simple — and not yet trustworthy.

The trouble is that the photocathode doesn't only release electrons when light hits it. At room temperature, electrons occasionally boil off on their own (thermionic emission). Each one starts the same million-fold avalanche as a real photon, so the tube emits a pulse with no particle present at all. These are dark pulses (or dark counts), and a bare counter fires them continuously — often thousands per second. Stray radioactivity, cosmic rays, and electronic pickup add still more false triggers.

So a single counter that fired tells you almost nothing: maybe a particle went by, maybe an electron hiccupped off the photocathode. You cannot tell which from one pulse.

Two counters that don't: coincidence building up

Here is the fix, and it is beautiful in its simplicity. Put two counters one behind the other and demand that they fire at the same time. A real particle flies through both within a nanosecond, so both bark together. A dark pulse in one tube is a private accident — the other tube knows nothing about it, so they almost never fire together by chance.

Requiring two (or more) counters to fire together is called a coincidence, and it is the single most powerful noise-killer in the business. If counter A dark-rate is a few thousand per second and so is B, the rate at which they fire accidentally within the same nanosecond window is their rates multiplied by that tiny window — a handful per second, or less. Real particles, which fire both on purpose, sail through.

In RADiCAL

The beam trigger is exactly this: small scintillator paddles and a 1×1 cm "finger" counter, required in coincidence, define a clean beam particle. Only when they all fire together does the experiment agree to record an event. You'll meet that decision again in Chapter 2.

✓ Check yourself

A bare scintillation counter clicks ~3000 times a second with the beam turned off. What are those clicks — and what single change makes almost all of them vanish?

Show answer
They're dark pulses: electrons that boiled off the photocathode on their own (thermionic emission) and set off the same million-fold avalanche as real light. Put a second counter in coincidence and require both to fire within a nanosecond — a dark pulse in one tube has no partner in the other, so the accidental rate collapses to a handful per second while real particles (which fire both) pass straight through.

Calibrating & characterizing a counter the real work

Before a counter is a measuring instrument rather than a curiosity, you have to characterize it — learn its personality — and calibrate it — pin its arbitrary numbers to physical ones. Three classic measurements do most of the job:

  • The single-photoelectron spectrum. Shine almost no light and histogram the pulse charges. You see distinct bumps: one photoelectron, two, three. The spacing between bumps is the charge of one photoelectron — that's your gain, measured, and it tells you how to convert "picocoulombs" into "photons."
  • The high-voltage plateau. Sit a counter in a steady particle source, put it in coincidence with a second, and sweep the HV while counting. At low voltage the gain is too small and pulses fall under threshold; the rate climbs as you raise HV, then flattens into a plateau where essentially every real particle is counted; push higher and dark counts take over and the rate runs away. You set the operating voltage in the middle of that plateau — high enough to be fully efficient, low enough to stay quiet.
  • Efficiency and noise rate. With a clean tagged sample (say, a tight coincidence of other counters), ask how often this counter also fired — that fraction is its efficiency. With the beam off, count how often it fires anyway — that's its dark/noise rate. A good counter is efficient and quiet at the same voltage.
Key idea

"Calibrate" and "characterize" are the difference between a detector and a number generator. The plateau tells you where to operate; the single-PE spectrum tells you what the numbers mean; the efficiency tells you how much to trust them. Skip these and every downstream result inherits an unknown error.

✓ Check yourself

You raise a PMT's high voltage by 10% and its pulses roughly double in size. Why is the gain so sensitive — and what does that force on the HV supply?

Show answer
Gain compounds: each of ~10 dynodes multiplies by 3–4, so the total is that per-stage factor raised to the tenth power. Nudging the voltage lifts every stage at once, so a small change in HV moves the gain steeply. That's the knob you use to set the gain on the plateau — but it also means the supply must be very stable, or the gain (and every charge you measure) drifts under you.
Where we are: we can now turn a single invisible particle into a trustworthy electrical pulse, and we know how good that pulse is. But a pulse is still an analog squiggle. Next we teach the experiment to make decisions from these pulses in nanoseconds — without a computer in the loop. → Chapter 2: NIM logic.
CHAPTER 2

Deciding in nanoseconds: NIM crates & logic

A particle arrives, your counters fire, and you have perhaps a few hundred nanoseconds to decide "is this worth recording?" — far too fast for software. The answer is a kind of analog computer built from standardized modules that do one logical operation each, in hardware, at the speed of light in a cable.

The NIM standard and its crate foundational

NIM — Nuclear Instrumentation Module — is a 1960s standard that has outlived almost everything else in physics because it is so simple. It fixes the size of a module, the connector that feeds it power, and the voltages on that connector. Any NIM module from any decade slides into any NIM crate (or "bin"), a metal frame with a power supply in the back. You build an experiment's fast logic by sliding in a handful of modules and patching their fronts together with short coax cables.

NIM modules speak a shared electrical language, NIM fast logic: a logic-0 is 0 volts and a logic-1 is −0.8 volts (a current into 50 Ω). The levels are negative and the pulses are a few nanoseconds wide on purpose — small, fast, and standardized so any module understands any other.

Discriminator: from analog squiggle to clean bit foundational

The first job is to convert each counter's messy analog pulse into a clean yes/no. That's a discriminator: it watches the input and, the instant the pulse crosses a threshold you set, it emits one standard NIM logic pulse of fixed width. Below threshold — small noise, dark pulses that didn't multiply much — nothing comes out.

A discriminator does two things at once: it rejects small junk (anything under threshold) and it standardizes what's left (every real pulse becomes the same crisp bit, regardless of how big it was). After a discriminator, the analog world is gone and you're doing pure logic.

Key idea — the catch

A simple discriminator fires when the rising edge crosses a fixed voltage. A taller pulse reaches that voltage earlier than a short one of the same shape, so the logic pulse comes out at a time that depends on pulse height. This amplitude-dependent timing error is time-walk — harmless if you only want to count, poison if you want to measure time. We come back to its cure (constant-fraction timing) in Chapter 3.

✓ Check yourself

A discriminator turns both a 50 mV and a 500 mV pulse into the same standard logic bit — but the two bits come out at slightly different times. Name the effect, and say when it's harmless and when it's fatal.

Show answer
Time-walk. A taller pulse crosses the fixed threshold earlier than a short one of the same shape, so the firing time depends on amplitude. Harmless if you only want to count particles — a bit is a bit. Fatal if you want to time them to picoseconds. The fix is to time at a constant fraction of each pulse's own peak (CFD), not a fixed voltage.

AND, OR, NOT, and the gate building up

With every counter reduced to a standard bit, you combine bits with more NIM modules:

  • Coincidence (AND). Output a pulse only if all inputs overlap in time. This is the hardware version of the two-counter trick from Chapter 1 — "A and B fired together."
  • OR. Output if any input fires — useful to fan several counters into one line, or to ask "did any veto paddle see something?"
  • Veto (NOT / anti-coincidence). A counter you require to not fire. Surround your good region with veto paddles and demand their silence, and you reject particles that strayed where they shouldn't.
  • Delay and gate width. Because logic is "do these overlap in time," when and how long each pulse lasts is part of the program. A few nanoseconds of cable delay lines two signals up; the discriminator's output width sets how wide the coincidence window is.

String these together — discriminate each counter, AND the ones you want, veto the ones you don't — and you have computed, in a few nanoseconds of cable and silicon, the single most important bit in the experiment: the trigger.

The trigger: "record this one" building up

The trigger is the logic signal that says now — capture this event. It is what defines, at the most basic level, what "an event" even is: no trigger, no data. Everything downstream — the digitizer, the file on disk, the analysis — only ever sees the events the trigger chose to let through.

In RADiCAL

The trigger is a coincidence of the beam scintillators and the finger counter: TRIGGER = ScintA · ScintB · Finger · ¬Veto. When a clean beam electron fires all three and misses the veto, the logic emits one pulse — and that pulse gates the digitizer in Chapter 3, telling it to freeze and record the waveforms. (There's a live version of exactly this logic in the field-guide Playground.)

✓ Check yourself

Why does the trigger ScintA · ScintB · Finger · ¬Veto keep far cleaner events than just requiring ScintA alone?

Show answer
Each AND'd coincidence multiplies the random rates by the tiny overlap window, so demanding three counters fire together drives accidental and dark coincidences to almost nothing — while a real beam particle fires all three on purpose. The veto then discards particles that strayed into the wrong region. One counter can't tell a particle from a hiccup; four wired in the right logic can.
Where we are: the experiment can now decide, in hardware, which particles are worth keeping. But a yes/no bit throws away everything about the pulse — its size, its shape, its precise time. To measure energy and picosecond timing we have to record the pulse itself. → Chapter 3: the DAQ.
CHAPTER 3

Recording the pulse: ADCs, QDCs & waveform digitizers

The trigger said "yes." Now the data-acquisition system — the DAQ — has to turn the analog pulses into numbers on a disk. There are three increasingly powerful ways to do it, and which you choose shapes what physics you can extract afterward.

The anatomy of a pulse foundational

Strip a signal down and it is just voltage versus time: a flat baseline (the "pedestal"), then a fast edge, a peak, and a decaying tail. Almost everything you'd want to know lives in two numbers. The amplitude (height of the peak) tracks the peak rate of light. The charge — the area under the pulse, ∫V dt — tracks the total light, which is what's proportional to deposited energy. A third, subtler quantity is hidden in the edge: when it crossed, the particle's time.

The three readout technologies below each grab a different subset of these: charge only, amplitude only, or — the whole thing — the shape itself.

The classic readout: ADC, QDC, TDC building up

Before digitizers were fast, you measured exactly the one number you wanted with a dedicated module, and the trigger told it when to look:

  • Peak-sensing ADC (analog-to-digital converter): catches the pulse's peak voltage and reports it as a number — the amplitude.
  • QDC (charge-to-digital converter, a "Q-ADC"): the trigger opens a gate — a time window of fixed width — and the QDC integrates the current that flows during it. The result is the charge, i.e. the area under the pulse inside the gate. This is the traditional way to measure a calorimeter's energy.
  • TDC (time-to-digital converter): a stopwatch. It reports the time between a start (often the trigger) and a stop (a discriminator firing on your channel).

These are cheap, fast, and give you small, tidy data — one number per channel per event. Their weakness is that they are committed in advance: a QDC's gate position and width are set in hardware before the run, and if a baseline shift or an early reflection sneaks into the gate, you can never go back and undo it. You measured charge; you did not keep the pulse.

✓ Check yourself

You measured a calorimeter's energy with a QDC, then realize the integration gate opened 5 ns too late and clipped the pulse's rise. With a QDC, can you recover the right charge? With a waveform digitizer?

Show answer
With a QDC, no — it integrated a fixed window in hardware and saved only that one number; the rest of the pulse is gone. With a waveform digitizer, yes — the whole shape is on disk, so you simply re-integrate with the correct window offline. That "decide afterward" freedom is exactly what the extra data buys you.

The waveform digitizer: keep everything the powerful way

A waveform digitizer refuses to commit. Instead of one number, it records the entire pulse shape — hundreds of voltage samples marching across the pulse — and ships that to disk. With the full waveform in hand you can compute charge, amplitude, and time offline, any way you like, and re-do it next year with a better algorithm. The data are bigger and the electronics trickier, but you have thrown nothing away.

In RADiCAL

Timing to picoseconds demands the waveform. The experiment uses a CAEN DT5742 built on the DRS4 chip — a switched-capacitor array that parks the voltage into 1024 tiny capacitors in turn at 5 giga-samples per second (one sample every 0.2 ns). Keeping the full edge is what lets us run a constant-fraction time pick in software (curing the time-walk from Chapter 2) and reach 27 ps. The price is a quirk — the capacitors aren't perfectly evenly spaced and sampling stops on a rotating "stop cell" — which has to be calibrated out, the kind of detail the report's DRS4 section is devoted to.

Key idea

QDC/ADC ask you to know what you want before the run. A waveform digitizer lets you decide after — at the cost of more data and more care. Whenever the measurement is hard (picosecond timing, odd pulse shapes, pile-up), keeping the whole waveform is worth it.

Where we are: we can detect a particle, decide to keep it, and faithfully record its pulses. The last question is the biggest: how do you turn those pulses into the particle's energy? → Chapter 4: calorimetry.
✓ Check yourself

Why does picosecond timing essentially force you to keep the full waveform, rather than just reading a single time off a TDC?

Show answer
A TDC gives you one time from a hardware discriminator — which carries time-walk and is locked to a single algorithm. Picosecond timing needs the shape of the edge: you run a constant-fraction pick in software and interpolate between the 0.2 ns samples for sub-sample precision — and you can improve the algorithm next year. Only the full waveform lets you do any of that.
CHAPTER 4

Weighing a particle in light: calorimetry

A counter says a particle was here. A calorimeter says how much energy it carried — by stopping it completely and measuring the light its destruction makes. This is where everything so far comes together, and where RADiCAL's design choices live.

The electromagnetic shower foundational

A high-energy electron or photon doesn't deposit its energy gently. In dense matter an electron radiates a photon (bremsstrahlung); that photon converts into an electron-positron pair; those radiate more photons — and the whole thing cascades, doubling the number of particles at each step until they're individually too feeble to multiply further. This avalanche of e⁺e⁻ and photons is an electromagnetic shower.

The natural ruler for a shower is the radiation length X₀ — the depth over which an electron loses most of its energy to radiation. A shower grows for the first several X₀, peaks, then fades; to catch essentially all of a high-energy shower you need roughly 20–25 X₀ of material. Tungsten's X₀ is only ~3.5 mm, which is why a tungsten calorimeter can be remarkably short.

Homogeneous calorimeters: the whole block glows building up

The most accurate way to measure a shower's energy is to make the entire absorber out of material that also makes light, so every bit of the shower contributes to the signal. A block of lead glass (radiating Cherenkov light) or a large scintillating crystal (like the PbWO₄ of CMS, or NaI) is a homogeneous calorimeter. Because nothing of the shower is invisible, the statistical fluctuations are minimal and the energy resolution is superb.

The cost is literally that: such crystals are expensive, heavy, and bulky, slow to grow, and often not radiation-hard — they darken under the dose of a hadron collider. A monolithic lead-glass wall is a gorgeous spectrometer and a logistical nightmare.

Sampling calorimeters: take a census building up

The alternative is to split the two jobs between two materials. Stack a dense, cheap, radiation-hard absorber (lead, tungsten, iron) to make the shower, and interleave thin active layers (scintillator, silicon, gas) to sample it. You only see the shower where it crosses an active layer — a few percent of it — and scale up. This is a sampling calorimeter.

The trade is fundamental. You only counted a sample of the shower, and samples fluctuate, so a sampling calorimeter pays a stochastic term in its resolution — the a/√E part of σ_E/E = a/√E ⊕ b — that a homogeneous device largely avoids. In exchange you get a detector that is compact, affordable, segmentable, and tough enough to survive a collider. For most large experiments that bargain is worth it.

✓ Check yourself

Lead glass and a tungsten/scintillator sandwich both measure a shower's energy. Which has the larger stochastic (a/√E) term, and why?

Show answer
The sampling sandwich. A homogeneous block turns essentially the whole shower into signal, so its energy estimate barely fluctuates. A sampling calorimeter only sees the shower where it crosses an active layer — a few percent of it — and those sampling fluctuations add statistical noise that scales as a/√E. You trade resolution for compactness, cost, and radiation hardness.

Why RADiCAL is a sampling calorimeter putting it together

RADiCAL is a sampling calorimeter by design, and a clever one. Its absorber is tungsten (tiny X₀, so the module is short) and its active medium is LYSO — a scintillator chosen because it is fast and bright enough to time, not just to weigh. The plates are stacked shashlik-style (skewered by wavelength-shifting capillaries that pipe the light out to SiPMs), and each corner is read at two gains: a high-gain tap at shower-maximum for timing, a low-gain tap over the full length for energy.

The goal isn't the best possible energy resolution — a homogeneous crystal would win that. It's a radiation-hard, compact, fast-timing calorimeter that can survive the brutal environment of a future collider and still tag a shower's arrival time to tens of picoseconds. That is a deliberate trade: accept the sampling term in the energy resolution to buy speed, robustness, and size.

In RADiCAL

The prototype here is only ~14 mm ≈ 4 X₀ deep — it ends before the shower even peaks, so a lot of energy leaks out the back. That's why its energy resolution shows a large constant term (the b above): it's a short test module, not a full-depth calorimeter. The timing, not the energy, is the headline — and the timing is 27 ps.

✓ Check yourself

RADiCAL's prototype is ~4 X₀ deep, but a 150 GeV shower peaks around 6–7 X₀. Predict the dominant feature of its energy resolution, and why.

Show answer
A large constant term (the b in σ_E/E = a/√E ⊕ b). The module ends before the shower even maxes out, so a big — and event-to-event variable — fraction of the energy leaks out the back. That leakage doesn't shrink with energy, so it shows up as an energy-independent floor. It's why this prototype's headline is its timing, not its energy.

Part II
The RADiCAL analysis
From recorded waveforms to a 27-picosecond result: the techniques this experiment applies, the traps it sidesteps, and the numbers it gets. This is where the lessons of the real analysis live.
CHAPTER 5

From a waveform to a row of numbers

Every triggered event is eight calorimeter waveforms plus the reference channels. Before any physics, each waveform has to become a few honest numbers — a baseline, an amplitude, a charge, a time. Get this bottom layer wrong and nothing built on top of it can be right.

Pedestal first foundational

A digitizer's "zero" isn't zero. Each channel sits at some small, slowly drifting baseline voltage — the pedestal — set by the electronics, not the particle. So the very first step on every waveform is to measure that baseline from the quiet samples before the pulse (here, the first ~50 samples) and subtract it. Every amplitude and charge you quote afterwards is a difference from the pedestal, not an absolute voltage.

Amplitude, charge, and the two gains foundational

With the baseline gone, two numbers fall out of each pulse. The amplitude (peak height; our SiPM and MCP pulses dip negative, so amplitude = baseline − minimum) tracks the peak rate of light and drives the timing. The charge — the area under the pulse — tracks the total light, which is what's proportional to deposited energy.

RADiCAL reads each corner at two gains so it never has to choose: a high-gain (HG) tap, tuned to be big and fast at shower-maximum, does the timing; a low-gain (LG) tap, which stays unsaturated over the full energy range, does the energy. The sum of the low-gain charges, ΣLG, is the experiment's energy and shower-containment proxy.

The time pick: constant fraction, in software building up

Because the whole waveform was saved (Chapter 3), the time is picked offline. RADiCAL uses CFD-5%: find where the pulse first crosses 5% of its own peak on the rising edge, interpolating between the 0.2 ns samples for sub-sample precision. Timing at a constant fraction rather than a fixed voltage is what defeats the time-walk from Chapter 2 — the crossing lands at the same spot on the edge no matter how big the pulse is. Each channel's time is recorded relative to the MCP reference.

In RADiCAL

The product of all this is the ntuple — one tidy row per event: x_trk, y_trk (where the wire chamber says it hit), sum_lg and sum_pb (calorimeter and Pb-glass energy), hg_peak[8] and lg_peak[8] (per-channel amplitudes), hg_cfd05[8] (per-channel CFD times vs the MCP), plus mcp_peak and a wc_ok quality flag. Everything in the rest of the analysis is cuts and fits on that one table.

✓ Check yourself

Why bother measuring a pedestal from the first ~50 samples instead of just assuming the baseline is 0 V? And which number — amplitude or charge — would you histogram to estimate a shower's energy?

Show answer
The baseline is a small, drifting electronic offset, not zero; since amplitude and charge are both measured relative to it, an unsubtracted or wrong pedestal biases every one of them. For energy you use the charge (the area, which is proportional to total light) — specifically the low-gain sum ΣLG. Amplitude is for timing.
Where we are: every event is now a row of clean numbers, including a CFD time for each channel measured against the MCP. But the MCP has jitter of its own — so how do we time to 27 ps through an imperfect reference clock? → Chapter 6: the corner difference.
CHAPTER 6

Timing without a perfect clock: the corner difference

This is RADiCAL's signature move, and the single idea most worth understanding. The hardest truth in picosecond timing is that you can only ever measure a time difference — and the reference you measure against has jitter of its own. The corner difference turns that liability into nothing at all.

You can only measure a difference foundational

There is no absolute "when." A time only means something relative to another clock. So every channel's CFD time is really t_i = T_i − T_MCP — the channel's true time minus the reference time the MCP provides. That's unavoidable, and it has a sting: whatever jitter the MCP reference carries gets stamped onto every single channel. Time one channel against the MCP and its width is dominated by the reference, not by the calorimeter you actually care about.

The (DW−UP)/2 corner difference the key technique

Now the trick. Take two readouts that share the same reference and the same digitizer group — a down-corner and an up-corner of the module — and subtract them:

(t_DW − t_UP) = (T_DW − T_MCP) − (T_UP − T_MCP) = T_DW − T_UP

The MCP term — and all of its jitter — cancels exactly, because it was common to both. What's left depends only on the two calorimeter channels. The same cancellation quietly kills two other gremlins: the DRS4 timebase error (both channels are digitized in the same group, on the same cells) and the beam-arrival jitter (common to the whole event). One subtraction, three noise sources gone. This is a reference-free time estimator.

In RADiCAL

This is precisely why the headline is MCP-free and reaches 27 ps. The DRS4 per-cell-width error is real — about 68 ps — yet it never touches the result because it cancels in the corner difference, a claim the report's time-base section verifies directly. There's a live version in the field-guide Playground: crank up the "MCP jitter" slider and watch the single-channel width balloon while the (DW−UP)/2 width doesn't move.

The √2 penalty that becomes a √2 prize building up

Subtracting isn't free: if each channel has timing jitter σ, their difference has jitter √2·σ (independent errors add in quadrature). But the estimator is (t_DW − t_UP)/2, and dividing by two scales the spread by a half: √2·σ / 2 = σ/√2. So the same operation that cancels the reference also averages two independent channels — you end up better than a single channel by √2, not worse. Cancel the clock and average the detector, in one stroke.

✓ Check yourself

Two channels each time against the MCP. Show the MCP drops out of (t_DW − t_UP) — and name one other error the same subtraction removes. Then: if each channel has jitter σ, what is the jitter of (t_DW − t_UP)/2?

Show answer
(T_DW−T_MCP) − (T_UP−T_MCP) = T_DW − T_UP — the common MCP term cancels exactly, taking all its jitter with it. The same cancellation also removes the DRS4 timebase error and the beam-arrival jitter (both common to the pair). The difference of two independent σ's is √2·σ; dividing by 2 gives σ/√2 — a √2 improvement over one channel.
Where we are: we have a reference-free, noise-cancelling time estimator. But a good estimator on the wrong events still lies. The last technical hurdle is choosing which events to keep — without fooling ourselves. → Chapter 7: honest selection.
CHAPTER 7

Choosing a cut without fooling yourself

Every resolution number is really "resolution, given these cuts." The danger is that the data has so many knobs — which region, which energy slice, which fraction — that you can always find a combination that looks brilliant by pure luck. This chapter is the discipline that keeps 27 ps honest, and it's the hardest-won lesson in the whole project.

The fiducial: trust the middle foundational

Showers near the edge of a small module leak sideways and time poorly, so the first cut keeps only events whose track landed in a clean central region — the fiducial. Crucially, that region is centred on the module, found from its physical edges (where the energy plateau falls off), not on wherever the beam happened to sit — the beam was actually steered a couple of mm off-centre. Center on the detector, not on the beam.

Energy binning and the best-contained bin building up

Not every event in the fiducial is equal: the best-contained showers — the ones that dumped the most of their energy in the module — also time the best. So we sort events by ΣLG into energy bins and report the resolution of the best bin, disclosed with the fraction of events it keeps. This is standard practice in calorimeter timing; the honesty is in stating the efficiency, not in pretending you used every event.

The overfitting trap, and out-of-sample the hard lesson

Here is where it gets dangerous. Scan the fiducial radius and the energy bins together and you can find a combination that reads as low as ~25.6 ps. It's tempting to quote it. But with that many knobs, some of that "improvement" is just the selection latching onto a lucky downward fluctuation — you've overfit the noise, and the number won't reproduce.

The cure is out-of-sample validation: choose the cut on one half of the data (the training half), then measure it on the other half it never saw (the test half). Luck doesn't survive the hand-off; only real structure does. When we do this here, the flashy in-sample minima degrade by anywhere from +2 to +26 ps out-of-sample — proof they were partly mirages. The selection that is stable out-of-sample lands at 27 ps, with a measured bias of essentially zero.

Quote σ_core — and own the tails building up

One more honesty knob: which width do you call "the resolution"? A timing distribution is a sharp Gaussian core plus a small non-Gaussian tail. The field-standard fast-timing metric is the width of the fitted Gaussian core (σ_core) — it's robust and it's what other experiments quote, so it's the fair number to headline. But you don't get to hide the tail: you report it separately — here, ~2.6% of events sit beyond 2.5σ, a benign shoulder that's the intrinsic shape of the per-channel CFD times, not a broken channel or time-walk. Headline the core; disclose the tail.

✓ Check yourself

Your radius-×-bins scan turns up a combination reading 25.6 ps. Why might that be a lie, and what single test settles it? And why quote σ_core plus a tail fraction rather than one raw RMS?

Show answer
With many knobs, the scan can latch onto a lucky downward fluctuation — overfitting. The test: out-of-sample validation — pick the cut on a training half, measure on a held-out half; if the number holds, it's real (here the in-sample minima inflate by up to +26 ps, so they weren't). You quote σ_core because it's the robust, field-standard metric a single tail event can't yank around, and you report the tail fraction separately so the headline isn't quietly hiding a shoulder. A single raw RMS blends the two and is neither comparable nor honest.
Where we are: reconstruction, a reference-free estimator, and a selection that survives its own scrutiny. Put them together and the experiment yields a small, specific set of numbers. → Chapter 8: what to expect.
CHAPTER 8

What the numbers say

Stack the apparatus, the corner trick, and the honest selection and the experiment delivers a handful of numbers. Here's what they are, how to read them, and — just as important — what not to over-claim about them.

The timing headline foundational

The result is σ_t ≈ 27 ps at 150 GeV: the Gaussian-core width of the (DW−UP)/2 corner estimator, on CFD-5% times, in the best-contained energy bin of the central fiducial — and validated out-of-sample, so it's a number a real operator could reproduce, not a lucky slice. For scale, light travels only ~8 mm in 27 ps; the detector pins a particle's arrival time better than the time it takes light to cross the module.

The resolution curve and its floor building up

Timing improves with energy, because a bigger shower makes more light and a faster edge. The trend fits the standard form σ_t = a/√E ⊕ b — a stochastic term a/√E that shrinks as the light grows, in quadrature with a constant floor b. For RADiCAL it's about 200/√E ⊕ 22 ps: ~47 ps at 25 GeV falling to ~27 ps at 150 GeV.

The floor is an extrapolation, not a verdict read it carefully

It's tempting to treat the floor b as "the detector's ultimate timing." It isn't — it's σ_t as E → ∞, a number beyond the highest energy anyone measured (150 GeV). And in this fit form a and b are strongly anti-correlated (ρ ≈ −0.8): a steeper stochastic term buys a lower floor for the same measured curve. So when a prior RADiCAL paper quotes a 17.5 ps floor and we fit 22 ps from the same 25–150 GeV data, that's a fit-decomposition difference, not a performance gap — both pass through ~27 ps at 150 GeV, and where data actually exist this analysis is equal or better. Quote the floor; just don't mistake an extrapolation for a measurement.

Energy resolution: the expected "disappointment" building up

The energy resolution, by contrast, looks poor — a large constant term in σ_E/E = a/√E ⊕ b. That is not a flaw; it's the 4 X₀ leakage from Chapter 4 showing up exactly where the physics says it should. This prototype was built to time, not to weigh. Reading the energy resolution as "bad calorimeter" misses the point; reading it as "short, leakage-dominated timing demonstrator" is correct.
