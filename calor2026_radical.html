Motivation

Future experiments demand more from calorimeters

  • Extreme pileup at future colliders → many overlapping interactions per crossing
  • Precision timing (≈10 ps) tags each shower in time → "4D/5D" calorimetry untangles them
  • Harsh radiation environments → radiation-hard optical materials are essential
  • Compact & high-resolution — energy, position and time, at once

No single material does it all

Speed, light yield, density, radiation hardness, and cost pull in different directions.

→ systematic, comparative R&D is needed.

Motivation (1:00). The driver is timing: pileup at future machines forces us to time showers to ~10 ps to associate them with the right vertex. But you also need radiation hardness, compactness, and good energy resolution — and no one material optimizes all of those. That tension is exactly what RADiCAL is built to explore.

The detector

The RADiCAL module — the size of an index finger

  • Sampling EM calorimeter: interleaved very-dense W absorber + dense scintillator plates
  • Scintillator: LYSO:Ce crystal plates today → LuO:Yb ceramic plates next
  • Assembled to 25 X₀ deep → contained EM showers in a tiny volume
  • ~14 × 14 mm transverse — a high-density, finger-sized timing calorimeter
The module (2:15). Picture a metallic index finger. Tungsten makes it dense and short; the scintillator plates make light; we read that light out longitudinally. 25 radiation lengths means the shower is contained in something you can hold between two fingers. The material is the knob we turn — LYSO now, ceramics next.
The readout

One structure → timing, position and energy

  • WLS filaments in quartz capillaries penetrate the module as waveguides
  • Light sampled at selected depths → longitudinal segmentation
  • SiPMs at both ends of each capillary
  • Timing layer near shower max → timing; dual-ended → position; full sampling → energy
Readout (3:00). The clever part. Capillaries with wavelength-shifting filaments act as waveguides through the stack, sampling light at chosen depths. Read both ends with SiPMs and the same structure gives you time (a fast layer at shower max), position (left–right and the dual-ended difference), and energy. One detector, three observables.

The vision

RADiCAL as a "standard candle" for calorimetry R&D

  • A well-characterized, fast, high-resolution reference platform
  • Swap in one variable at a time: scintillator, WLS material, SiPM, front-end, DAQ
  • Measure each against a known fast-timing + energy baseline
  • Reproducible yardstick → apples-to-apples comparisons across the community
Standard candle (4:15) — the thesis of the talk. This is the message I want people to walk out with. Because RADiCAL is fast and well-understood, it's a controlled environment: change one thing — a new ceramic, a new WLS, a new SiPM, a new DAQ — and the detector tells you, quantitatively, whether it's better. A reproducible yardstick the whole community can use. Linger here.

Methods

Getting to picoseconds

① CFD timing

Time at a constant fraction of each pulse (CFD-5%) → amplitude-independent, kills time-walk.

② Corner trick

The (DW−UP)/2 difference cancels the shared timing reference → an MCP-free estimator.

③ Honest selection

Energy-binned by containment, cross-validated out-of-sample → no optimization bias.

Next two slides: see ① and ② live.

Methods (5:45). Three ideas. CFD for amplitude-independent timing. The corner difference to cancel our own reference clock — that's how we get a reference-free number. And rigorous, cross-validated selection so the result is honest. I'll show the first two interactively.
Live · technique ①

Why CFD, not a fixed threshold

● fixed-threshold time walks with amplitude · ● CFD (5% of peak) stays locked.

CFD demo (6:15). Drag the amplitude. Watch the red line — a fixed threshold — slide earlier as the pulse grows. That's time-walk, and it would wreck our resolution. The green line, CFD at 5% of the peak, doesn't move. That's why we time at CFD-5%.
Live · technique ②

Why (DW−UP)/2 beats the reference jitter

Crank the MCP jitter: the single channel balloons; the (DW−UP)/2 estimator doesn't move.

Corner demo (6:45). Now crank up the jitter of the reference clock. A single channel's resolution blows up — pink. But the corner difference (DW minus UP, over two) sits still — the common reference cancels exactly. This is how we measure the detector's intrinsic timing without being limited by our reference. It's the heart of the 27 ps result.

Current plans · electronics & DAQ

Toward 10 ps in the readout

Timing budget

σt ≈ noise / slew-rate

→ lower noise and faster, brighter pulses both buy picoseconds.

  • SiPMs: higher PDE, faster single-photon response
  • Front-end: lower-noise, higher-bandwidth amplification
  • DAQ: next-generation digitizers / sampling
  • End-to-end co-design of material + sensor + electronics
Electronics (11:15). Timing resolution is noise over slew rate — so we attack both: better SiPMs and front-end for lower noise and faster edges, and a next-gen DAQ. The point is co-design: the material, the sensor, and the electronics have to be optimized together, and the platform lets us do that one variable at a time.

Backup · timing method

The (DW−UP)/2 corner technique

  • Each channel measures ti = Ti − Tref (referenced to the MCP)
  • tD − tU = TD − TU → reference cancels exactly
  • ÷2: turns the √2 subtraction penalty into a √2 averaging gain → σ = σch/√2
  • Also cancels the DRS4 timebase error and beam-arrival jitter
Backup · rigor

Why the number is trustworthy

  • Reference-free: corner difference removes the MCP entirely
  • Cross-validated: selection chosen on one fold, σ measured out-of-sample → no optimization bias
  • Beam-independent geometry: module centre from calorimeter edges, not the beam centroid
  • Full waveforms: every timing choice re-derivable offline
