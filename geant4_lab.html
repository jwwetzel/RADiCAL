A simulation is a hypothesis you can run. Before (and after) a beam test you want to predict how much light a shower makes, where it makes it, when the photons arrive, and what the readout chain does to that signal. This lab builds that chain in GEANT4 — the toolkit CERN uses to push particles through matter — one layer at a time, and ends by producing a waveform in exactly the format the Data Lab taught you to read.

CH 1

Calorimetry, from the top

A calorimeter measures a particle's energy by destroying it: the particle dumps all its energy into the detector as a cascade of secondaries, and we count the result. An electron or photon develops an electromagnetic shower — high-energy electrons radiate photons (bremsstrahlung), high-energy photons convert to e⁺e⁻ pairs, and the two feed each other until the particles drop below a critical energy Ec and the shower dies.

One length scale governs all of it: the radiation length X₀ — the depth over which an electron loses all but 1/e of its energy, and roughly the distance between shower generations. In tungsten X₀ ≈ 3.5 mm; in LYSO ≈ 11 mm. Two rules of thumb follow:

  • Longitudinal: the shower peaks at depth tmax ≈ ln(E/Ec) radiation lengths and needs ~25 X₀ to contain fully. RADiCAL's prototype is only ~4 X₀ — deliberately short, so it leaks, which is why its energy resolution has a large constant term (you saw this in the paper).
  • Transverse: the shower's width is set by the Molière radius RM — ~90% of the energy lands within one RM of the axis. This is why the capillaries sit where they do.

RADiCAL is a sampling calorimeter: it alternates a dense absorber (tungsten, to make the shower develop in a short space) with thin active layers (LYSO scintillator) that actually produce light. Only a small sampling fraction of the shower energy is deposited in the active material, and the shot-to-shot fluctuation in how much lands there is the dominant stochastic term in the resolution:

σ_E / E = a / √E ⊕ b / E ⊕ c (stochastic) (noise) (constant: leakage, non-uniformity)
Why we simulate

Every quantity above — sampling fraction, shower max, leakage, light yield, and the arrival times of the photons that set the 27 ps timing — is a prediction. A simulation lets you compute it for a geometry you haven't built yet, and check the one you did build against the data.

CH 2

What we want out of the simulation

Decide the observables first — they drive every later choice (which physics to enable, how finely to model the optics, how many events to run). For RADiCAL the list maps one-to-one onto the test-beam measurements:

Simulation outputReal-detector observable it predicts
Energy deposited in active LYSOSampling fraction & the energy-resolution stochastic term
Longitudinal & transverse profilesWhere shower-max is → where to place the timing capillaries
Optical photons created per eventLight yield & photo-statistics (the floor on timing)
Photon arrival-time distribution at the SiPMPulse rise time → timing resolution σt
Detected charge & waveformThe DRS4 trace, dual-gain HG/LG, saturation

The headline RADiCAL number — ~27 ps — comes from the last two rows. Timing precision scales as σt ≈ (rise time) / √Npe: a fast edge and many detected photoelectrons. A simulation that gets the photon count and photon arrival times right can predict that number; one that only tracks deposited energy cannot. That is why a chunk of this lab is about optical photons, not just energy.

Scope your physics to your question

Studying energy resolution? You can skip optical photons entirely and score energy deposits — fast. Studying timing? You must generate and track every optical photon to the SiPM — slow, but it is the only way to get arrival times. Know which one you're doing before you press run.

CH 3

Monte Carlo & statistics

A shower is a branching random process: at each step a particle might radiate, convert, or scatter, with probabilities set by physics cross-sections. There is no closed-form answer for "what does one 100 GeV electron do" — so we don't compute it, we sample it. That is the Monte Carlo method: draw random numbers, follow the cross-sections, and let one simulated particle be one possible history. Run many, and the distribution of histories is the answer.

Two consequences you must keep in mind:

  • Statistical error falls as 1/√N. The uncertainty on a mean (mean energy, mean σt) shrinks like 1/√Nevents. To resolve a 2 ps effect you need enough events that your statistical error is well under 2 ps — quadrupling the events only halves the error.
  • Optical photons are expensive. LYSO makes ~30,000 photons per MeV. A shower depositing a GeV in the active layers is tens of millions of optical photons, each individually tracked through reflections and absorption. Optical timing runs are the slowest thing you will do — budget for it, and use variance reduction (e.g. simulate a representative fraction of photons and scale) when you can.
Always fix the seed

Monte Carlo is random, but it must be reproducible. Set the random-number seed explicitly (G4Random::setTheSeed(12345)) so a run can be repeated bit-for-bit — essential for debugging and for comparing two geometries fairly. Change the seed (not the geometry) to get an independent statistical sample.

Check yourself
Your timing run gives σt = 31 ± 4 ps from 500 events; a colleague's different geometry gives 27 ± 4 ps. Have you shown the second geometry is better?

Answer: No. The two differ by 4 ps with ~6 ps combined error — consistent within statistics. Either run far more events (error ∝ 1/√N, so ~16× more events to reach ±1 ps), or you cannot claim a difference. This is the same 1/√N logic that forced the out-of-sample treatment in the real analysis.
CH 4

GEANT4 setup & anatomy

Install. The least painful routes on a Mac/Linux box are conda (conda install -c conda-forge geant4) or a source build following the GEANT4 install guide; either way, also install the physics data files and source the environment script so GEANT4 can find them.

The run hierarchy. Everything is organised as Run → Event → Track → Step: a run is many events (beam particles), each event is the tree of tracks (the primary plus every secondary), and each track advances in steps. You hook into this tree to collect data.

The three mandatory pieces. You must supply: a detector construction (geometry + materials), a physics list (which processes are active), and a primary generator (the beam). The skeleton:

main.cc
#include "G4RunManagerFactory.hh" #include "FTFP_BERT.hh" // a standard EM + hadronic physics list #include "G4OpticalPhysics.hh" #include "DetectorConstruction.hh" #include "PrimaryGenerator.hh" int main(int argc, char** argv) { auto* run = G4RunManagerFactory::CreateRunManager(); run->SetUserInitialization(new DetectorConstruction()); // geometry + materials (Ch 5,7,8) auto* physics = new FTFP_BERT(); // showers: brem, pair, ionisation... physics->RegisterPhysics(new G4OpticalPhysics()); // scintillation, Cherenkov, WLS (Ch 6) run->SetUserInitialization(physics); run->SetUserAction(new PrimaryGenerator()); // the electron beam run->Initialize(); run->BeamOn(1000); // fire 1000 showers delete run; return 0; }

The geometry lives in DetectorConstruction::Construct() (next three chapters); the beam lives in a G4VUserPrimaryGeneratorAction that fires, say, 150 GeV electrons with a G4ParticleGun; and you collect results in optional user action classes (Run/Event/Stepping), which we use for the readout in Chapter 9.

CH 5

Geometry & materials

GEANT4 geometry is three nested objects: a solid (the shape), a logical volume (shape + material + readout), and a physical volume (a logical volume placed inside a mother volume). Everything lives inside one big world volume. Materials come from the NIST database, or you build them from elements — LYSO isn't in NIST, so we mix it ourselves.

DetectorConstruction::Construct() — materials and the world
auto* nist = G4NistManager::Instance(); G4Material* tungsten = nist->FindOrBuildMaterial("G4_W"); // absorber, X0 ~ 3.5 mm G4Material* vacuum = nist->FindOrBuildMaterial("G4_Galactic"); // the world // LYSO is not a NIST material — build it from elements (illustrative fractions): G4Material* lyso = new G4Material("LYSO", 7.1*g/cm3, 4); lyso->AddElement(nist->FindOrBuildElement("Lu"), 71.4*perCent); lyso->AddElement(nist->FindOrBuildElement("Si"), 6.4*perCent); lyso->AddElement(nist->FindOrBuildElement("O"), 18.2*perCent); lyso->AddElement(nist->FindOrBuildElement("Y"), 4.0*perCent); // World: a box of vacuum (half-lengths in each dimension) auto* worldSolid = new G4Box("World", 10*cm, 10*cm, 30*cm); auto* worldLV = new G4LogicalVolume(worldSolid, vacuum, "World"); auto* worldPV = new G4PVPlacement(nullptr, {}, worldLV, "World", nullptr, false, 0);

Now one absorber plate and one scintillator tile, placed back to back — the unit cell we repeat later:

// 3.5 mm tungsten = one radiation length auto* absSolid = new G4Box("Abs", 7*mm, 7*mm, 1.75*mm); // half-lengths auto* absLV = new G4LogicalVolume(absSolid, tungsten, "Abs"); new G4PVPlacement(nullptr, {0,0,-2.0*mm}, absLV, "Abs", worldLV, false, 0); // 1.5 mm LYSO tile, just downstream of the absorber auto* sciSolid = new G4Box("Sci", 7*mm, 7*mm, 0.75*mm); auto* sciLV = new G4LogicalVolume(sciSolid, lyso, "Sci"); new G4PVPlacement(nullptr, {0,0,+0.5*mm}, sciLV, "Sci", worldLV, false, 0);
Two beginner traps

Half-lengths: G4Box takes half the size in each dimension — a "7 mm" box is 14 mm wide. Overlaps: placed volumes must not intersect their siblings or poke outside their mother; run with /run/initialize then /geometry/test/run (or pass checkOverlaps = true to G4PVPlacement) to catch overlaps before they corrupt your physics.

CH 6

Light: scintillation vs Cherenkov vs WLS

So far we deposit energy. To get light we must turn on optical photons and tell each material how it makes and moves them. Three distinct processes matter, and RADiCAL uses all of them:

The three processes

  • Scintillation. Ionisation energy excites the crystal, which relaxes by emitting photons — isotropic, with a yield proportional to deposited energy and a characteristic decay time (LYSO ~40 ns). This is the bulk of the light. The number of photons is Poisson-distributed, which sets the photo-statistics floor on timing.
  • Cherenkov. A charged particle moving faster than light-in-the-medium (β > 1/n) emits a faint, directional, and essentially instantaneous cone of photons. Few photons, but their prompt timing makes them valuable for the sharpest edges.
  • Wavelength shifting (WLS). A dopant in the capillary absorbs the blue/UV scintillation light and re-emits it isotropically at a longer (green) wavelength after a short delay. Re-emitted green light is trapped by total internal reflection and piped down the fibre to the SiPM. This is how the light gets out of the stack and to the sensor.

You enable all three by registering G4OpticalPhysics (Chapter 4); GEANT4 then activates G4Scintillation, G4Cerenkov, and G4OpWLS wherever a material's properties support them. Those properties live in a G4MaterialPropertiesTable attached to each material:

optical properties — LYSO as a scintillator
// Photon energies that bracket the emission band (~2.0–3.5 eV ≈ 350–620 nm) std::vector<G4double> E = { 2.0*eV, 2.5*eV, 3.0*eV, 3.5*eV }; std::vector<G4double> rIdx = { 1.81, 1.81, 1.82, 1.82 }; std::vector<G4double> absL = { 40*cm, 40*cm, 40*cm, 40*cm }; std::vector<G4double> emit = { 0.1, 1.0, 0.6, 0.1 }; // emission spectrum shape auto* mpt = new G4MaterialPropertiesTable(); mpt->AddProperty("RINDEX", E, rIdx); // required for ANY optics (and Cherenkov) mpt->AddProperty("ABSLENGTH", E, absL); // bulk absorption length mpt->AddProperty("SCINTILLATIONCOMPONENT1", E, emit); // shape of the emitted spectrum mpt->AddConstProperty("SCINTILLATIONYIELD", 30000./MeV); // ~30k photons / MeV mpt->AddConstProperty("SCINTILLATIONTIMECONSTANT1", 40.*ns); // LYSO decay time mpt->AddConstProperty("RESOLUTIONSCALE", 1.0); // 1.0 = pure Poisson stats lyso->SetMaterialPropertiesTable(mpt); lyso->GetIonisation()->SetBirksConstant(0.126*mm/MeV); // light saturates at high dE/dx

The capillary core gets WLS properties instead — an absorption length for the blue it eats, an emission spectrum for the green it makes, and a re-emission time constant:

optical properties — a wavelength-shifting capillary core
auto* wls = new G4MaterialPropertiesTable(); wls->AddProperty("RINDEX", E, { 1.59, 1.59, 1.59, 1.59 }); // fibre core index wls->AddProperty("WLSABSLENGTH", E, { 5*mm, 2*mm, 0.4*mm, 0.1*mm });// absorbs blue strongly wls->AddProperty("WLSCOMPONENT", E, { 1.0, 0.6, 0.1, 0.0 }); // re-emits green wls->AddConstProperty("WLSTIMECONSTANT", 8.*ns); // re-emission delay capCore->SetMaterialPropertiesTable(wls);
RINDEX is the master switch

No RINDEX → no optical photons, full stop. Scintillation needs RINDEX + SCINTILLATIONYIELD; Cherenkov needs only RINDEX (it fires for any fast charged track); WLS needs WLSABSLENGTH + WLSCOMPONENT. Define the property only on materials that should glow — giving the tungsten a RINDEX would (wrongly) make it scintillate and tank your runtime.

Check yourself
You add RINDEX to LYSO but forget SCINTILLATIONYIELD. Run a 150 GeV electron — do you get photons?

Answer: Yes, but only a handful of Cherenkov photons (RINDEX alone enables Cherenkov), not the ~millions of scintillation photons you expected. A nearly-dark detector with a few prompt photons is the classic symptom of a missing SCINTILLATIONYIELD.
CH 7

Constructing a single tile

Now assemble the unit cell: the absorber + LYSO pair from Chapter 5, plus a capillary — a thin cylinder of WLS core threaded through the tile — and a reflective wall so light is piped along it rather than lost. A cylinder is a G4Tubs (inner radius, outer radius, half-length, start/sweep angle):

a scintillating/WLS capillary through the tile
// A 0.5 mm-radius capillary running along z, in the NE corner of the tile auto* capSolid = new G4Tubs("Cap", 0, 0.5*mm, 0.75*mm, 0*deg, 360*deg); auto* capLV = new G4LogicalVolume(capSolid, capCore, "Cap"); new G4PVPlacement(nullptr, {3*mm, 3*mm, 0}, capLV, "Cap", sciLV, false, 0); // Make the capillary wall reflective so green light is trapped and piped to the SiPM end auto* surf = new G4OpticalSurface("capWall"); surf->SetType(dielectric_metal); // mirror-like boundary surf->SetFinish(polished); surf->SetModel(unified); auto* surfMPT = new G4MaterialPropertiesTable(); surfMPT->AddProperty("REFLECTIVITY", E, { 0.95, 0.95, 0.95, 0.95 }); surf->SetMaterialPropertiesTable(surfMPT); new G4LogicalSkinSurface("capSkin", capLV, surf);

That is a complete, glowing unit cell: a shower particle crossing the LYSO makes blue scintillation light; some of it enters the capillary, gets wavelength-shifted to green, reflects off the polished wall, and travels to the end face where (Chapter 9) a SiPM waits. Optical surfaces are as important as bulk materials here — the difference between a polished and a rough wall, or 95% vs 98% reflectivity, changes your light yield and your timing.

A modelling choice to make explicit

Here the capillary segment sits inside one tile. In the real detector a single capillary runs the full length of the module, sampling every layer — we build that in the next chapter. Decide early whether your capillary is per-tile (simple, good for light-yield studies) or continuous (needed for realistic timing, since photon transit time down the fibre matters).

CH 8

Constructing the module

A module is the unit cell repeated. The clean way is a loop that places each absorber + scintillator pair at its own z (GEANT4's G4PVReplica can do a periodic stack in one call, but an explicit loop is easier to read and to vary):

stack the shashlik layers
const int nLayers = 28; // ~4 X0 of W in this short prototype const G4double pitch = 3.5*mm + 1.5*mm; // one absorber + one scintillator const G4double z0 = -0.5 * nLayers * pitch; for (int i = 0; i < nLayers; ++i) { G4double z = z0 + i * pitch; new G4PVPlacement(nullptr, {0,0, z }, absLV, "Abs", moduleLV, false, i); new G4PVPlacement(nullptr, {0,0, z + 2.5*mm }, sciLV, "Sci", moduleLV, false, i); }

Then thread the eight timing capillaries through the whole stack, in the four-corner pattern of the real readout — each corner sampled at an upstream depth (“Up”) and at shower-max (“Down”). The copy number is how the readout (Chapter 9) tells the eight capillaries apart:

auto* longCap = new G4Tubs("LongCap", 0, 0.5*mm, 0.5*nLayers*pitch, 0*deg, 360*deg); auto* longCapLV = new G4LogicalVolume(longCap, capCore, "LongCap"); const G4double r = 3*mm; // corner offset from the axis const G4double corner[4][2] = { {+r,+r}, {+r,-r}, {-r,-r}, {-r,+r} }; // NE, SE, SW, NW for (int c = 0; c < 4; ++c) { new G4PVPlacement(nullptr, {corner[c][0], corner[c][1], 0}, longCapLV, "Cap", moduleLV, false, c); // copyNo 0..3 } // (In RADiCAL each corner is read at two depths; here one continuous capillary // per corner keeps the example short — split it into Up/Down segments to mirror // the (DW−UP)/2 corner estimator from the analysis.)
Geometry is a knob, not a constant

Because the module is built from a few parameters — nLayers, pitch, absorber thickness, capillary radius and position — you can scan them. “What does 6 X₀ instead of 4 buy in energy resolution?” or “does moving the capillary 1 mm toward the axis improve timing?” are one-line changes plus a re-run. That is the whole point of having a model.

CH 9

Simulating the SiPM, the circuit, and noise

GEANT4 will deliver optical photons to the end of each capillary. Turning those into a realistic waveform takes two stages: a sensitive detector that catches the photons inside GEANT4, and a SiPM + electronics model that you apply afterward.

9.1 Catch the photons — a sensitive detector

Attach a G4VSensitiveDetector to the capillary end face. Its ProcessHits runs for every step inside that volume; we keep only optical photons and record which capillary and when the photon arrived — the arrival-time list is the raw material for timing.

SiPMSD::ProcessHits — record photon arrival time & capillary id
G4bool SiPMSD::ProcessHits(G4Step* step, G4TouchableHistory*) { auto* track = step->GetTrack(); if (track->GetDefinition() != G4OpticalPhoton::OpticalPhotonDefinition()) return false; // only optical photons auto* hit = new SiPMHit(); hit->capId = step->GetPreStepPoint()->GetTouchable()->GetCopyNumber(); hit->time = track->GetGlobalTime(); // arrival time [ns] hit->wavelen = 1239.8*eV / track->GetTotalEnergy(); // nm, for the PDE curve fHitsCollection->insert(hit); track->SetTrackStatus(fStopAndKill); // photon absorbed at the photocathode return true; }

At end-of-event you now have, per capillary, a list of photon arrival times — an ideal, noise-free, 100%-efficient sensor. Real SiPMs are none of those things.

9.2 The SiPM & electronics model

A SiPM is a grid of tiny Geiger-mode photodiodes (microcells). Each detected photon fires one cell into a fixed-size avalanche; the output is the sum over fired cells. The model adds, in order: PDE (only a fraction of photons are detected), dark counts (cells fire thermally with no light), optical cross-talk (one avalanche can trigger neighbours), afterpulsing, and saturation (a finite number of cells). Then the electronics convolve the fired-cell times with a pulse shape, split into the high- and low-gain chains, add electronic noise, and the DRS4 samples it. Done as a post-processing step on the photon list:

sipm.py — photon arrival times → a digitized waveform
import numpy as np def make_waveform(arrival_ns, *, pde=0.40, gain_mV=2.0, dcr_GHz=5e-5, xtalk=0.15, tau_rise=1.0, tau_fall=20.0, fs_GHz=5.0, n=1024, noise_mV=1.3, rail_mV=820.0): a = np.asarray(arrival_ns) # 1. Photon detection efficiency: keep a random fraction a = a[np.random.random(a.size) < pde] # 2. Optical cross-talk: each fired cell may trigger neighbours (extra cells, same time) a = np.repeat(a, 1 + np.random.poisson(xtalk, a.size)) # 3. Dark counts: random fires spread over the digitizer window window = n / fs_GHz # ns n_dark = np.random.poisson(dcr_GHz * window) a = np.concatenate([a, np.random.uniform(0, window, n_dark)]) # 4. Convolve each fired cell with the single-cell pulse shape (bi-exponential) t = np.arange(n) / fs_GHz # sample times [ns] wave = np.zeros(n) for t0 in a: s = (t >= t0) wave += s * gain_mV * (np.exp(-(t - t0)/tau_fall) - np.exp(-(t - t0)/tau_rise)) # 5. Electronic noise — the ~1.3 mV DRS4 baseline RMS, then clip at the rail (saturation) wave += np.random.normal(0.0, noise_mV, n) wave = np.clip(wave, None, rail_mV) return t, wave

To get the dual-gain readout, call this twice with two gains: a high gain_mV that clips at the rail (the fast, saturated timing channel) and a low one that stays linear (the energy channel) — exactly the HG/LG split the paper dissects. Vary pde and the scintillation yield to study how the timing floor moves with light; clip vs don't-clip to study saturation.

Now close the loop

The output of make_waveform is a 1024-sample trace in millivolts — the same shape as one channel of a real RADiCAL event. Feed it to the same CFD-5% and (DW−UP)/2 code from the Data Lab and you can measure a simulated σt with the identical pipeline used on the beam data. Simulation and measurement become directly comparable — which is the entire point of building the model.

CH 10

Run it, and where to go next

With the three mandatory classes in place, ./radical_sim fires BeamOn(N) and writes, per event, the deposited energy and the per-capillary photon lists. From there:

  • Validate against data. Compare three numbers to the beam test before trusting anything else: the sampling fraction, the photo-electron yield per GeV, and σt at 150 GeV (target ~27 ps, paper). If those match, the model has earned the right to make predictions.
  • Then predict. Scan the knobs from Chapter 8 — depth in X₀, capillary radius and position, SiPM PDE, scintillator light yield — and watch energy and timing resolution respond.
  • Analyse simulated waveforms with real code. Write the make_waveform output into the same array layout the Data Lab reads, and run those macros unchanged.
The habit to keep

A simulation is only as trustworthy as its weakest validated link. Build the chain one box at a time (Ch 1→9), check each against something you can measure, and never quote a simulated number whose inputs you haven't pinned to data. A model that reproduces the 27 ps is a tool; one that hasn't been checked is a guess with good production values.

From here, the natural path back is the Data Lab (analyse what you simulate), the findings paper (the numbers to match), and the GOING RADiCAL primer (the physics underneath it all).
