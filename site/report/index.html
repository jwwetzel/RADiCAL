★ Headline result The MCP-free (DW−UP)/2 estimator on the brightest-1000 events reaches 25.7 ± 0.6 ps at 150 GeV (DSB1 build, srCFD), with ≈50 ps (50.5 ps) across the full timing fiducial beside it. It is robust by construction: the corner difference cancels the per-group timing reference (≈73 ps inter-group jitter), the DRS4 cell-width error, and the position walk alike.
The brightest-1000 selection is deterministic — the 1000 largest-ΣLG fiducial events at each energy, fixed before any fit — so there is no trained bin choice and no optimization bias by construction (0.7% of fiducial events at 150 GeV, 3.4% at 25 GeV; selection-systematic ±1.0 ps from K/r/veto variations). Timing uses the per-regime estimator rule: srCFD (the 5% leading edge, recovered from the LG channel when the HG pulse clips) for the high-light builds, LED for the dim builds. The σt-vs-E curves for all four builds are in Layer 5.
High-energy limit Fitting σt = a/√E ⊕ b to the production curve gives a = 203 ± 6 ps·√GeV and an asymptotic timing floor of 18.8 ± 0.8 ps — consistent with, and confirming, the published 17.5 ps constant term of arXiv:2401.01747 (NIM A 1068 (2024) 169737). The floor is an extrapolation beyond the data: our lowest measured point is 25.7 ps at 150 GeV. Its plausible physical origin is longitudinal shower-depth fluctuation — timing shifts by -33.6 ± 2.9 ps per e-fold of collected light (the "depth dial", Layer 5) — not a DAQ artefact.

Layer 1 -- Hardware Integrity

Before any physics, one question: is the instrument sound? RADiCAL is a W/LYSO shashlik prototype read out by eight high-gain capillaries (fast → timing) and eight low-gain capillaries (slow → energy) on a DRS4 digitiser; the May 2023 CERN-SPS H2 run took electrons at 25–150 GeV. We walk the readout from the ground up — are the channels alive, are the pulses clean, can we trust the clock, is the response linear — so every number in the layers that follow rests on a verified detector.

Are all eight channels alive — and quiet?

Pedestal noise (baseline RMS) of each capillary against the 5 mV noise floor, with every channel's active fraction.

★ Key FindingYes — all eight capillaries are live and quiet. Pedestal RMS ≈ 1.3 mV, far below the 5 mV floor; the active fraction stays high on every channel (>94% even for the weakest); saturation is negligible. NW-Up carries the weakest signal of the eight but is fully functional — flagged here and explained later by light yield, not a dead channel.

They fire — but are the pulses clean and consistent?

Average pulse shape of all 16 readout channels at 150 GeV (normalised): the fast high-gain capillaries (timing) and the slow low-gain capillaries (energy).

★ Key FindingEvery channel produces a clean, consistent pulse. The HG chain is fast (a sharp prompt peak in the first ≈18 ns — good for timing); the LG chain is AC-coupled, so each pulse is followed by a balancing undershoot (≈35%) that recovers to baseline by ≈500 ns with no ringing — stable baseline restoration across the whole module.

The pulses are clean — but can we trust the clock to picoseconds?

The DRS4 samples on 1024 capacitors with a rotating start (“stop”) cell. This is the stop-cell coverage against the ideal uniform diagonal.

★ Key FindingYes — the time base is sound, and its residual is corrected. The stop cell rotates uniformly (every physical cell is sampled equally → a data-driven correction is well-posed). The per-cell width calibration was not applied (nominal 0.2 ns/cell), but that uncalibrated width is common-mode and cancels exactly in the (DW−UP)/2 corner estimator — so it does not limit the headline. The residual stop-cell pattern is corrected and validated out-of-sample on the MCP-referenced combination (A²-combo 120.7 → 99.5 ps, split-half).

Timing is sound — is the energy response linear?

Mean signal amplitude vs beam energy for each of the eight capillaries, 25–150 GeV.

★ Key FindingYes — the response is linear and the channels track together. All eight capillaries scale with energy in lock-step. The high-gain chain compresses near its rail above ~50 GeV — by design, since energy is measured on the linear low-gain channels — and the low-gain chain (right) stays straight across the full 25–150 GeV range, hugging the proportional y ∝ E reference. That is the chain energy is actually read out on, so the linearity that matters is confirmed. No channel gains anomalously less per GeV. The instrument is verified; on to the timing reference.

How do we know the time base is sound? (the full study)

The DT5742 was read with a nominal time axis (0.200 ns/cell), so the only DRS4 timing artefact that can matter is the fixed per-cell width error. Two facts settle whether it touches the headline: the stop cell rotates uniformly (left), so a data-driven correction is well-posed; and a split-half out-of-sample test (right, trained on even events, tested on odd) shows that correction helps only the MCP-referenced combination, while the (DW−UP)/2 corner is already immune. The full per-group cell-width spectra and correction-pattern fits are in the Layer-1 appendix. Generated by drs4TimeBase.C.

★ Key FindingThe DRS4 cell-width error is real (~68 ps, common-mode across DRS0-G0 channels) but CANCELS in the (DW-UP)/2 corner estimator (same group, same crossing cell) and in MCP1-MCP2 -- so the production headline is immune to it, and the 73 ps inter-group reference jitter is genuine. It DOES limit the MCP-referenced combination methods (mean-all, A^2-weighted-all): split-half out-of-sample validation shows the A^2 combo improving 120.7 -> 99.5 ps after correction. Does the digitiser stay clean across all six energies?

The DRS4 noise floor (pedestal RMS) for all eight channels across the full 25–150 GeV scan, against the 5 mV threshold; saturation and spike rates (both flat at zero) are stated. Distilled by layer1Summary.C from drs4Diagnostics.C.

★ Key FindingYes — the DRS4 stays clean at every energy. The noise floor is flat at ~1.3 mV across all channels and all six energies, far below the 5 mV threshold; HG saturation is <0.1% even at 150 GeV (energy is read on the unsaturated low-gain chain); the worst spike rate is ~0.2%. No channel degrades with beam rate or energy.

Are the channels independent — or is there cross-talk?

If two channels shared an electronic path, their pulse amplitudes would track each other event-by-event. The 8×8 matrix below is the Pearson correlation ρ of every high-gain channel pair at 150 GeV — the single cleanest test of whether the channels are independent. Generated by channelIntegrity.C.

★ Key FindingThe channels are independent — the correlations are physics, not electronics. Every pair is strongly correlated (ρ = 0.86–1.00) because one EM shower lights the whole capillary bundle at once — common-mode shower sharing, not cross-talk. The tell: the least-correlated channel is SE-D (the geometric outlier), exactly as shower geometry predicts; and subtracting a neighbour makes the per-sample residual noise worse, not better, which rules out a shared electronic path.

Do the pulse shapes hold up across all six energies?

A clean time base is necessary but not sufficient — the pulse shape itself must stay invariant as the energy climbs, or the CFD timing point would walk. Each panel below overlays all six beam energies for one HG capillary (mean ± RMS band, 5000 DRS4 waveforms each), aligned on that channel's CFD-20% crossing (t = 0, dashed red). Produced by averageWaveforms.C reading the raw pulse TTrees directly — waveforms are not stored in the compact ntuple.

★ Key FindingThe pulse shape is energy-invariant. All eight HG capillaries produce well-formed negative-going pulses whose shape is fixed across 25–150 GeV — only the amplitude grows with energy. That is exactly what a stable CFD timing point requires. SE-U's lower amplitude is a reduced light yield (WLS-fibre coupling), not a distorted waveform — its shape matches the others.

Is the response uniform across the beam spot?

Energy-invariant shape still leaves one question: does each capillary respond the same regardless of where in the beam spot the particle lands? Two views answer it — the mean HG amplitude versus beam energy per channel (left), and the HG/LG amplitude-ratio maps across the impact position at 150 GeV (right, inverted-kRust COLZ, binned at the canonical 1 mm WC resolution). ★ Key FindingThe response is position-independent within the fiducial. The HG/LG ratio is flat across the beam spot for every capillary — no geometric shadow, hot, or dead region. The amplitude-vs-energy curves confirm all eight scale together, with SE-U and SW-U running ~10–15% low (longer upstream WLS-fibre path) and a gentle high-energy roll-off as the high-gain stage saturates — which is exactly why the energy measurement is anchored on the low-gain channels.

Layer 2 -- Reference Characterization

Status: COMPLETE -- timing reference characterized. All capillary timing measurements are made relative to the MCP timestamp (hg_cfd[i] = t_capillary -- t_MCP). Understanding the MCP's own timing jitter is essential: it sets a noise floor for per-channel measurements but cancels exactly in the (DW--UP)/2 combination, which is why that estimator achieves sub-MCP resolution.

Reference characterization at a glance

The two “MCPs” are one MCP passively split into the two DT5742 readout groups (verified: amplitude correlation = 1.000), so each group has its own copy of the same time reference. Expand the panel below for the full per-channel and per-energy detail.

Which capillary limits the timing — and is it the same one at every energy?

The per-channel jitter above is a single number; this map breaks it open. Every cell is the single-channel CFD-5% σt (ps) for one capillary at one energy — eight rows × six energies, the whole reference-limited landscape on one page (inverted-kRust: bright = worse). Produced by compareEnergies.C.

★ Key FindingThe limiting channel changes with energy — which is exactly why we combine corners rather than lean on any one channel. At 25 GeV the Down capillaries blow up (to ~330–380 ps single-channel): low amplitude means residual time-walk even low on the edge. By 100–150 GeV that walk is gone and the best channels reach ~160–185 ps. Two Up capillaries, NW-U and SW-U, stay warm at every energy (~225–260 ps) — the persistent light-yield laggards seen in Layer 1. No single channel is good enough alone; the (DW−UP)/2 corner average plus the brightest-1000 selection (Layers 4–5) is what turns this spread into the headline.

If every capillary followed the trend — the detector-potential projection

Two Up capillaries buck the single-channel trend (NW-U drifts up with energy, SW-U stays flat), and both are averaged into the headline (DW−UP)/2. A fair question: how much do they cost, and what would an all-uniform calorimeter time? We answer it two ways — an honest empirical test, and a clearly-labelled projection. Produced by idealUniform.C.

★ Key FindingThey are a modest handicap, not a liability — and the method is robust to them. First the counter-intuitive empirical fact: dropping the weak channels makes the headline worse, not better. Reconstructing (DW−UP)/2 from the raw waveforms at 150 GeV, the all-fiducial σt goes 67 ps with all four Up channels → 76 ps dropping NW-U → 101 ps dropping both — the 8-channel corner average benefits from every channel, even a mediocre one. So the weak channels should be kept. Second, the projection: if NW-U and SW-U instead followed the well-behaved Up trend, the headline would improve by ≈2 ps at 150 GeV (the exact projected values are annotated on the figure); the effect vanishes below ~75 GeV, where the two are no worse than the light-starved Down channels. This is an intrinsic-potential projection (the combination model is validated at high E: 69 vs 67 ps reconstructed at 150) — the measured 25.7 ps headline stands as the result.

Optimising the timing fiducial — a per-energy, OOS-validated cut

The hero above draws the energy fiducial at r < 2 mm (tight, for shower containment). The timing fiducial we set by explicit optimisation rather than assumption. Reusing the headline’s exact Method-A formula, event cuts, data-derived centroid and Gaussian-core fit, we scan the corner σt versus fiducial radius at all six energies, two complementary ways: (1) the joint optimisation — the actual single-best-Emeas-bin estimator re-selected at each radius (literally what sets the headline; our replica reproduces the per-energy σt to <0.5 ps), and (2) a stable selection — a fixed top-2% / top-10% by ΣALG that freezes the Emeas cut so the pure radius/position effect reads off a smooth curve. Crucially, every candidate is checked with the run-folded out-of-sample (OOS) validation — because a tighter circle shrinks the selection pool, the single best bin can overfit, and only the OOS exposes it. Historical role: this scan (run with the earlier best-Emeas-bin estimator) is the study that set the adopted per-energy radii; the production brightest-1000 chain inherits exactly this ramp (2.5 mm for E ≤ 100 GeV, 3.0 mm for E ≥ 125 GeV) and, being deterministic, needs no per-event bin choice of its own. Generated by fiducialTimingScan.C.

★ Key FindingThe timing fiducial is optimised per energy: r < 2.5 mm for 25–100 GeV, r < 3.0 mm for 125–150 GeV — every choice OOS-validated (optimisation bias 0.0 ps). Two regimes emerge from the physics. At the lower energies the outer-ring position/time-walk degradation dominates, so tightening to 2.5 mm robustly improves the OOS resolution (50/75/100 GeV better by 1.7–3.6 ps; 25 GeV tied). At the higher energies the larger, higher-amplitude samples let the single-best-bin benefit from the looser pool: at 125 GeV the tighter cut overfits (in-sample better but OOS clearly worse), and at 150 GeV 3 mm is OOS-optimal — so both keep 3 mm. Net effect: the adopted radius ramp carries over unchanged into the production chain — the OOS machinery here guarded the radius choice, and the brightest-1000 selection that now sits on top of it is deterministic, so no bin choice remains to overfit. This is a genuine, OOS-guarded optimisation — not a per-energy argmin. The last two figures make that explicit: the best-bin curve is jumpy because the top Emeas bin flickers across the N ≥ 500 line (a selection artifact, not physics), and a naive radius×bin minimisation reaches “15 ps” in-sample that the held-out data exposes as +2 to +26 ps of overfitting. The fixed-radius, OOS-validated headline is the value that survives data it was not tuned on — which is the only kind a referee, or a real experiment, should believe.

Wire-chamber spatial resolution & track binning

Data-driven resolution of the delay-line beam chamber, measured without a second tracker. Position is x = (7/36 mm/ns)·(tR−tL); the hit position lives in the difference of the two end-times, so the sum tR+tL is position-independent and its spread isolates the timing noise that limits the position (σx = 7/36 · σ(sum)). Reads raw waveforms (highest-energy run); generated by wireChamberResolution.C.

★ Key Findingσx ≈ 3.7 mm, σy ≈ 3.6 mm (peak-time, as x_trk/y_trk are built; ≈3.3 mm with sub-sample CFD-50%). Energy-independent (the sum method is position-blind). Beam spot for context: σx≈7.6 mm, σy≈5.2 mm. The ~mm resolution is set by peak-timing on a slow digitiser, not the wire pitch. Track-binning guidance: the position quantises in ~0.25 mm steps (one sample), so hit-map / beam-profile plots are fine at 1–2 mm; but for position-dependent analysis use bins ≥ the resolution (≈3.5–4 mm) — finer bins are correlated by the smearing, not independent measurements.

Per-channel transverse maps & the MCP-connector check

Mean pulse amplitude vs beam-track impact point for each of the 8 capillaries plus the 1×1-cm trigger, shown before (good track only) and after the 1×1 trigger (ch15 > 60 mV). Binned at 0.25 mm in x and 0.5 mm in y (finest-but-no-finer: xtrk is continuous, ytrk has a peak-sample comb below ~0.5 mm), inverted-kRust palette (signal dark on white). Generated by transverseMaps.C (150 GeV shown; all energies in Output/Summary).

★ Key FindingThe rings are pre-showering in the connector metal, not absorption — harmless to the calorimeter, so no connector veto is applied. Controlling for beam intensity (same radius from the beam centre, connector vs no-connector), the connectors enhance the 1×1 signal ~2.4× (SMA 391 vs 160 mV control; BNC barrel 407 vs 204 mV) — the opposite of absorption. A connector barrel is ~one radiation length of brass/steel in the beam: it starts the EM shower early (one e− → many e+e− + γ), and that multiplied swarm sprays into the small downstream 1×1 trigger. The ring shape is the coaxial geometry: the solid outer barrel (annulus) is the most material → brightest rim, while the thin central pin / dielectric showers less → a dip in the middle. Crucially, a shower conserves energy — pre-starting it a few cm upstream does not destroy energy, so the deep calorimeter still integrates the full shower. Consistent with this: the BNC region (~(7,5) mm, on the beam core) carries normal calorimeter energy (5402 vs 5549 mV) and a test veto there worsens the 150 GeV timing (by ~3 ps in the best-bin-era check) by discarding the highest-Emeas, best-contained showers — the very events the brightest-1000 selection keeps; the SMA (~(20,3) mm) lies outside the active square (⟨Σcap⟩ 1196 vs ~5500 mV) so the fiducial cut already removes it. The 1×1 trigger is retained only as a clean-beam (no-pedestal) selection — it removes the ~24% no-hit population, not the connectors.

Layer 4 -- Calibration

Status: COMPLETE -- walk corrections applied and cross-validated. The raw DRS4 waveform time is extracted at a programmable point on the leading edge; the production estimator is srCFD (the 5% edge, recovered from the LG channel when the HG pulse clips) for the high-light builds and LED for the dim builds. Calibration at a glance

The calibration and combination steps turn a ~180 ps single channel into the 25.7 ps production headline. Expand the panel below for the full per-energy method, CFD-fraction, walk-correction and cut-optimisation detail.

CFD-fraction optimisation (the timing “shoulder”)

Why the production estimator times at the 5% edge. The single most important per-channel timing lever is where on the rising edge the time is taken, not the walk correction. Group timing resolution (the four Down vs the four Up capillaries) is shown as a function of CFD fraction at all six energies. Estimator: each capillary is shifted to its own median MCP-referenced Δt (fiducial events, hg_peak > 20 mV) and the four are pooled; σt is the RMS within ±1.0 ns of the pooled median; error bars are statistical (σ/√2N, smaller than the markers).

★ Key FindingThe Down-capillary timing “shoulder” is a CFD-fraction / leading-edge effect, not a DRS4 satellite, not a selection artefact, and not correctable by an amplitude walk fit (verified in elbowInvestigation.C / walkCorrTest.C). The 5% edge — the common near-minimum across energies, and exactly where the production srCFD estimator times — removes it with no loss of events.

The mechanism: leading-edge shape jitter

Why the fraction matters — the cause, not just the consequence. The naive explanation (“20% sits on a slow part of the edge”) is wrong: the mean rising edge is actually steeper at 20% than at 5% (left), so electronic noise (σt ≈ σV/(dV/dt)) would make 20% better. The real cause is pulse-to-pulse shape variation (right).

★ Key FindingThe Down-capillary shoulder is leading-edge shape jitter that grows with threshold height — not the mean slope (which is steeper at 20%), not noise, not amplitude walk. Timing low on the edge (the 5% point — srCFD's operating point) samples the most reproducible part of the pulse. This is the cause behind the CFD-fraction trend above (generated by edgeMechanism.C).

The timing floor: 18.8 ± 0.8 ps — confirming the published 17.5 ps

The constant term b of the production σt(E) fit is 18.8 ± 0.8 ps — statistically consistent with, and therefore confirming, the 17.5 ps floor published in arXiv:2401.01747 (NIM A 1068 (2024) 169737) on the same detector and beam. The floor is σt as E → ∞ — an extrapolation beyond the highest measured point (150 GeV) — and its plausible physical origin is longitudinal shower-depth fluctuation: timing shifts by -33.6 ± 2.9 ps per e-fold of collected light (the depth dial). Generated by timingFloorComparison.C.

★ Key FindingThe production floor 18.8 ± 0.8 ps CONFIRMS the published 17.5 ps — it does not revise it. Both numbers are extrapolations beyond the 150 GeV data edge (our lowest measured point is 25.7 ps), fit on the same detector and beam, and they agree within uncertainties. The floor has a physical candidate: longitudinal shower-depth fluctuation moves the effective light-collection point along the WLS capillary, shifting the corner time by -33.6 ± 2.9 ps per e-fold of collected light — a geometry effect common to every build, not a DAQ or electronics artefact.

Is the quoted σ clean — what's in the tails?

We quote the Gaussian-core σ (the standard fast-timing convention), so a fair question is what lives in the non-Gaussian tail. For a bright-event regime of the same order as the production selection (150 GeV, r < 3 mm, top-2% by Emeas) we classify every event as core or tail (|t − μ| > 2.5σ) and test the three usual culprits — low amplitude (time-walk), too few valid channels, or a single rogue channel (CFD mis-reconstruction). Generated by timingTailAnalysis.C.

★ Key FindingThe core σ is clean. The distribution is a Gaussian core plus a modest non-Gaussian shoulder — ~2.6% of events beyond 2.5σ (a pure Gaussian gives 1.2%), which raises the trimmed RMS to ~33 ps versus the ~31 ps core, a ~2 ps effect. Those tail events have the same Emeas as the core (median 5128 vs 5119 mV) and the same worst single-channel deviation (225 vs 231 ps), so the tail is not time-walk and not a rogue channel — it is the intrinsic non-Gaussian shape of the per-channel CFD times, mildly enhanced by the few events missing a channel (95% vs 100% full-8). A negligible 0.13% sits beyond 250 ps — rare catastrophic mis-reconstruction a real experiment removes with a one-line quality cut. So the quoted Gaussian-core σ is the right, well-behaved figure of merit.
