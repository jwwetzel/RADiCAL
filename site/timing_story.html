Act I · The instrument and the clock

A calorimeter that also tells time

RADiCAL is a compact tungsten / LYSO:Ce shashlik electromagnetic calorimeter — 25 radiation lengths in ~135 mm. An electron shower deposits its energy as scintillation light, collected by four wavelength-shifting capillaries read out at both ends (Down and Up) — eight SiPM channels in all.

Every channel is recorded twice, at two gains. The high-gain (HG) copy is the fast timing channel; the low-gain (LG) copy measures energy and stays linear all the way to 150 GeV. Two CAEN DT5742 digitizers sample every waveform at 5 GS/s (1024 cells), with a micro-channel-plate PMT providing a common start t₀ and wire chambers tracking where each electron entered.

The timing observable is the depth half-difference (DW−UP)/2 — half the difference between the down-end and up-end shower times. The common start cancels, leaving a clean, self-referencing measure of how deep in the module the shower developed. Target resolution: 20–30 ps. The rest of this page is the story of getting there.

Act II · The wall

The timing channel saturates

The HG channel is digitized in a DC-offset window built to also catch the pulse's negative afterswing. The price: a hard ceiling. Every HG pulse clips flat at ~820 mV. At 25 GeV only a few percent of pulses reach it; by 75 GeV and above, 90–94% are clipped — the true peak is simply gone.

Constant-fraction discrimination (CFD) times a pulse at a fixed fraction of its peak. But if the peak you measure is the clip, the fraction is wrong — the crossing slides down to the low-slope foot of the pulse, exactly where timing is most fragile. The tell is unmistakable:

Every CFD fraction we tried — 3%, 5%, 10%, 20%, 30%, 50% — lands at ~28 ps at 150 GeV. None beats another, because they are all referencing the same clip. The resolution plateaus across 50–150 GeV: more light does not help.
Act III · The lever

The low-gain copy never clips

The same shower that saturates the HG channel lands, attenuated, in the LG channel — where it stays comfortably on-scale and linear to 150 GeV. HG and LG are one pulse seen at two gains, so they are tied by a single constant: HG_peak = a + b·LG_peak. Crucially, that gain ratio is a property of the electronics, not the shower — it must not depend on beam energy.

Act IV · The method

srCFD: put the threshold on the edge

The fix follows directly. For each event, predict the true (unclipped) HG peak from the low-gain copy, HG_true = a + b·LG_peak, and place the CFD threshold at 15% of that true peak. Because the true peak towers over the clip, 15% of it lands high on the steep rising edge — not down on the foot. We call this srCFD (saturation-recovered CFD; the code name is hg_lgcfd) — and it is the production timing estimator for the high-light builds.

Why 15%? Push the fraction to 30% and, for the brightest showers, the threshold rides into the clip itself and the timing blows up. 15% is the steep-but-safe sweet spot — ~98.5% of pulses yield a valid crossing. The recipe is calibrated once from clean, unclipped low-energy data (calibHGLG) and applied at every energy.

Act V · The payoff

The production headline: 25.7 ps, and a floor that confirms the published one

The production timing chain is srCFD on a deterministic brightest-1000 selection — the 1000 largest-ΣLG fiducial events at each energy, fixed before any fit, so there is no optimization bias by construction. For the high-light DSB1 build it reaches 25.7 ± 0.6 ps at 150 GeV, with ≈50 ps over the full fiducial sample at the same energy — the two are quoted together throughout, the best-measured showers beside the whole population.

Act V½ · Why a deterministic selection

A fixed count of the brightest, not a bin that can starve

An earlier version of this analysis read resolution by slicing events into energy bins and taking the best one — and a subtle failure lurked there: the top bin sits in the Gaussian tail, and that tail empties as energy rises, so at 150 GeV the brightest bin could fall below the minimum-count cut and be discarded, silently penalizing the highest energies. The production chain removes the freedom entirely: the brightest-1000 rank cut takes the same fixed count of the best-measured showers at every energy — no bin to starve, and no per-event selection left to overfit. That is why the headline needs no held-out cross-validation to defend it: the selection is fixed before any number is read.

Is the “floor” real?

The curve, fit to σt = a/√E ⊕ b, reports a floor b — the softest number on this page. It is an extrapolation (no beam exceeded 150 GeV; σt is still falling there), and a and b are strongly anti-correlated. So rather than trust a bare fit we let the data set the uncertainty: statistical errors σ/√(2N), a free fit, and error inflation by √(χ²/ndf). The result is b = 18.8 ± 0.8 ps, statistically consistent with the published 17.5 ps measured on the same detector — an independent confirmation, never a revision.

Act VI · What's left is physics

The floor is the shower, not the instrument

Why doesn't a 3.7× steeper edge give a 3.7× better resolution? Because by the time we average seven timing channels, the per-pulse slew term is already small — a few picoseconds. What remains in (DW−UP)/2 is something the electronics can't touch: the longitudinal depth of the shower maximum fluctuates event to event, and that half-difference is a measure of it. The signature is direct — the mean upstream–downstream asymmetry drifts by −33.6 ± 2.9 ps per e-fold in beam energy (the “depth dial”), consistent in sign and scale with the expected logarithmic migration of shower maximum. So the ~18.8 ps floor is shower-development physics, not a DAQ artefact. (This depth readout is CFD-based; a fixed-threshold crossing does not track the emission centroid, so the dial is a property of srCFD timing.)

So srCFD does precisely the right thing — it recovers the true rising edge that saturation hid — and the experiment then tells us, cleanly, where the remaining limit lives. We push the instrument until the detector itself is the thing we're measuring. That is the headline: not just a single number, but a method that takes the clipped foot off the table and leaves us face to face with the shower.

Bottom line. srCFD — CFD on the LG-predicted true peak — is the production timing estimator, and for DSB1 it reaches 25.7 ± 0.6 ps for the brightest-1000 showers at 150 GeV (≈50 ps across the full fiducial). The fitted floor 18.8 ± 0.8 ps confirms the published 17.5 ps — the same floor from an independent estimator. That residual floor is shower-depth fluctuation (−33.6 ps/e-fold) — the shower, not the instrument.
Act VII · The full spectrum

Four builds, one law: light yield, not WLS species

RADiCAL ran four builds — the LYSO:Ce scintillator and tungsten absorber common to every one, the wavelength-shifting (WLS) capillary the variable. Push the same production chain through all of them — brightest-1000 (DW−UP)/2 with the per-regime estimator (srCFD for the high-light builds, LED for the dim ones) — and they fall into a single clean ordering by scintillation light yield. More light → faster rising edge → better timing, exactly as the slew argument predicts.

The per-regime rule: why the dim builds use LED

The estimator is chosen by an ex-ante rule, not per-result. A plain low-fraction CFD (cfd05) fires at 5% of the pulse peak — on LuAG:Ce's small (~380 mV), slow, low-light pulses that 5% point sits low on a shallow, noisy edge, so the crossing time jitters badly. srCFD's edge recovery helps only when there is a clipped peak to recover; the dim builds rarely clip. So the rule is simple and fixed in advance: srCFD for the high-light builds (DSB1, MIXED), where saturation recovery is the win, and the fixed-threshold led for the dim builds (LuAG:Ce, TENERGY), which fires at a set level on the edge rather than a fraction of a tiny, noisy peak.

Read that way, LuAG:Ce gives a clean monotonic ladder — ~74 → 44 ps from 50 to 150 GeV. More light, better timing, as it must be. (LuAG:Ce's HG does clip at high energy, 2% rising to 83% — but LED handles it; clipping was never the dim-build's limiting problem.)

The proof: every bin is a clean Gaussian

Read with the production per-regime source and shown the way the publication does it (arXiv:2401.01747, Fig. 21), the data is unambiguous. For each build we pool all six energies, bin by shower amplitude (ΣLG), and Gaussian-fit the (DW−UP)/2 (MCP-free) and (DW+UP)/2 (absolute) distributions in every bin — fitting the central peak only (the satellite shoulder excluded, exactly as the publication does). They are clean Gaussians at every amplitude, and the bottom panel is their fitted σt — the distributions are sound across the whole light-yield range.

Resolution is set by light, not beam energy

Read σt in consecutive 1000-event brightness slices — brightest 1000, next 1000, … — separately per beam energy, and where the energies overlap in amplitude they land on one curve: timing is set by light yield, not beam energy. The width falls as 1/light — faster than the 1/√light of photostatistics — the signature of slew-rate timing (σt = noise/slope, slope ∝ amplitude). This is also why the headline is quoted both ways: the brightest-1000 selection (25.7 ps for DSB1) and the full-fiducial sample (≈50 ps) are two points on the same smooth σ(brightness) curve — the best-measured showers beside the whole population, both named (Act V).

The spectrum, in one line. Timing tracks light yield, top to bottom — 25.7 ps for the brightest DSB1 down to 44.4 ps for low-light LuAG:Ce — while DSB1 and LuAG:Ce capillaries reading the same showers agree within ~10–20% (ratio 1.04 ± 0.05). Light yield, not WLS species, sets the clock. The detector you build sets the clock you can read.
